radiation.synchrotron.SEDs.one_zone.SSA_SED_PowerLaw.from_physics_to_params#

SSA_SED_PowerLaw.from_physics_to_params(B: float | ndarray | Quantity, R: float | ndarray | Quantity, distance: float | ndarray | Quantity, *, p: float | ndarray = 3.0, f: float | ndarray = 0.5, theta: float | ndarray = 1.5707963267948966, epsilon_B: float | ndarray = 0.1, epsilon_E: float | ndarray = 0.1, gamma_min: float | ndarray = 1.0, gamma_max: float | ndarray = 1000000.0)[source]#

Compute the synchrotron self-absorption break frequency and peak flux.

This is a low-level, performance-optimized routine that assumes all inputs are provided as dimensionless scalars or NumPy arrays in CGS units. No unit validation or safety checks are performed.

The function analytically eliminates the normalization of the electron energy distribution using microphysical energy-partition assumptions, introducing a correction factor delta to account for the convergence of the electron energy integral when \(p < 2\).

Parameters:

  • B (float or array-like) – Magnetic field strength in Gauss.

  • R (float or array-like) – Radius of the emitting region in cm.

  • distance (float or array-like) – Distance to the source in Mpc.

  • p – See the user-facing function compute_BPL_SED_from_BR.

  • f – See the user-facing function compute_BPL_SED_from_BR.

  • theta – See the user-facing function compute_BPL_SED_from_BR.

  • epsilon_B – See the user-facing function compute_BPL_SED_from_BR.

  • epsilon_E – See the user-facing function compute_BPL_SED_from_BR.

  • gamma_min – See the user-facing function compute_BPL_SED_from_BR.

  • gamma_max – See the user-facing function compute_BPL_SED_from_BR.

Returns:

  • nu_brk (float or array-like) – Synchrotron self-absorption break frequency (Hz-equivalent CGS).

  • F_nu_brk (float or array-like) – Peak flux density at the break frequency (CGS-equivalent).

Notes

In the optically thick regime, the synchrotron SED follows a power law

\[F_\nu = \frac{c_5}{c_6} \left(B\sin\theta\right)^{-1/2} \left(\frac{\nu}{2c_1}\right)^{5/2} \frac{\pi R^2}{d^2}.\]

In the optically thin regime, the SED follows a different power law:

\[F_\nu = \frac{4\pi f R^3}{3 d^2} c_5 N_0 \left(B\sin \theta\right)^{(p+1)/2} \left(\frac{\nu}{2c_1}\right)^{-(p-1)/2}.\]

We define the break frequency \(\nu_{\rm brk}\) as the frequency where these two power laws intersect and the corresponding peak flux density \(F_{\nu_{\rm brk}}\). By equating the two expressions for \(F_\nu\) at \(\nu = \nu_{\rm brk}\), we can solve for \(\nu_{\rm brk}\) and \(F_{\nu_{\rm brk}}\) in terms of the physical parameters \(B\), \(R\), and \(d\).

Equation these two equations yields

\[\nu_{\rm brk} = 2 c_1 \left[\frac{4}{3} c_6 f N_0\right]^{2/(p+4)} R^{2/(p+4)} \left(B\sin\theta\right)^{(p+2)/(p+4)}\]

Inserting \(\nu_{\rm brk}\) back into either expression for \(F_\nu\) gives the peak flux density \(F_{\nu_{\rm brk}}\).

The normalization \(N_0\) of the electron distribution is eliminated analytically by equating the total electron energy density to a fraction \(\epsilon_E\) of the post-shock internal energy density, with magnetic energy fraction \(\epsilon_B\).

For \(p \neq 2\), this introduces a correction factor

\[\delta = \begin{cases} 1, & p > 2 \ (\gamma_{\max}/\gamma_{\min})^{2-p} - 1, & p < 2 \end{cases}\]

which accounts for the convergence properties of the electron energy integral.
