.. _chevalier_theory:

============================================
Theory: The Chevalier Self-Similar Solution
============================================

The Chevalier self-similar solution
(:class:`~triceratops.dynamics.shocks.chevalier.ChevalierSelfSimilarShockEngine`)
provides a physically motivated description of shocks generated by supernovae
and other explosive transients interacting with a surrounding circumstellar
medium (CSM). Originally derived by
:footcite:t:`chevalierSelfsimilarSolutionsInteraction1982` to model radio and
X-ray emission from supernovae, this solution has since become the foundational
framework for shock-powered emission modeling across a broad range of
astrophysical transients.

.. contents:: On This Page
   :local:
   :depth: 2

----

Overview and Assumptions
------------------------

The Chevalier solution describes the interaction between homologously expanding
supernova ejecta and an ambient medium under the assumption of self-similar
evolution.

.. admonition:: Model Assumptions

   - The **ejecta density** follows a power-law in velocity space:

     .. math::

           \rho(r, t) = A_1\, t^{-3} \left(\frac{r}{t}\right)^{-n}

   - The **CSM density** follows a power-law in radius:

     .. math::

           \rho_{\rm csm}(r) = A_4\, r^{-s}

   - **Self-similar dynamics** — the shock radius and velocity evolve as power
     laws in time; there is no intrinsic length or timescale.

   - **Strong, adiabatic shocks** — Rankine–Hugoniot jump conditions relate
     upstream and downstream thermodynamic quantities.

   - **Thin-shell approximation** — the forward and reverse shocks are
     sufficiently close that the shocked region may be treated as a single thin
     shell when computing radiative properties. While
     :footcite:t:`chevalierSelfsimilarSolutionsInteraction1982` solved the full
     self-similar ODE system for the detailed internal structure, the thin-shell
     approximation (following :footcite:t:`ChevalierXRayRadioEmission1982`) has
     since become standard practice for radiative modeling.

----

Self-Similar Scaling Relations
-------------------------------

In a self-similar framework, there is no intrinsic length or timescale, so all
ratios of physical quantities must remain constant over time. Fixing the ratio
of ejecta to CSM density at the shock radius constrains the time evolution of
the shock:

.. admonition:: Key Result: Shock Scaling

   .. math::
      :label: shock_radius

      R_{\rm sh}(t)
      = \left[\frac{A_4}{A_1}\,\zeta\right]^{1/(s-n)} t^{(3-n)/(s-n)}
      \equiv A_{\rm sh}\, t^{\lambda},

   where :math:`\zeta` is a dimensionless constant set by the solution, and

   .. math::

      \lambda = \frac{n-3}{n-s}.

   The shock velocity follows immediately:

   .. math::
      :label: shock_velocity

      v_{\rm sh}(t) = \frac{dR_{\rm sh}}{dt} = \lambda\,\frac{R_{\rm sh}}{t}.

The normalization constant :math:`A_{\rm sh}` encodes the detailed internal
structure of the shocked region and cannot, in general, be obtained in closed
form. This motivates the thin-shell approximation, which permits an analytic
expression for :math:`\zeta`.

----

Thin-Shell Normalization
-------------------------

.. admonition:: Key Result: Normalization Constant

   .. math::

      \zeta =
      \frac{
          3\lambda^2
          + 4\,\dfrac{(\lambda - 1)\lambda}{3-s}
      }{
          3(1-\lambda)^2
          - 4\,\dfrac{(\lambda - 1)\lambda}{n-3}
      }

   For a wind-like CSM (:math:`s = 2`) this simplifies to

   .. math::

      \zeta = \frac{(n-4)(n-3)}{2},

   consistent with :footcite:t:`ChevalierXRayRadioEmission1982`. With
   :math:`\zeta` in hand, the full shock dynamics are determined.

.. dropdown:: Derivation: Thin-Shell Momentum Balance
   :icon: book-open

   The net pressure force on the shell equals the rate of change of its
   momentum:

   .. math::

      \frac{d}{dt}\!\left[(M_2 + M_3)\,\dot{R}_{\rm sh}\right]
      = 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2 (P_3 - P_2).

   **Swept-up masses**

   The forward-shock mass (swept CSM) is

   .. math::

      M_3(R_{\rm sh}) = \frac{4\pi A_4}{3-s}\,R_{\rm sh}^{3-s}.

   The reverse-shock mass corresponds to ejecta with velocities exceeding
   :math:`\tilde{v} = R_{\rm sh}/t`:

   .. math::

      M_2(t)
      = \int_{R_{\rm sh}}^{\infty} 4\pi \xi^2 \rho_1(\xi,t)\,d\xi
      \simeq \frac{4\pi A_1}{n-3}\,t^{n-3}\,R_{\rm sh}^{3-n},
      \qquad n > 3.

   The total shell mass therefore scales as

   .. math::

      M_{\rm sh}
      = 4\pi \left[
          \frac{A_4}{3-s}\,R_{\rm sh}^{3-s}
          + \frac{A_1}{n-3}\,t^{n-3}\,R_{\rm sh}^{3-n}
      \right]
      = K_{\rm sh}\,t^{\gamma},
      \qquad
      \gamma = \frac{(n-3)(3-s)}{n-s}.

   **Post-shock pressures (strong-shock Rankine–Hugoniot)**

   .. math::

      P_2 = \frac{3}{4}\,\rho_1(R_{\rm sh},t)\,(\tilde{v} - \dot{R}_{\rm sh})^2,
      \qquad
      P_3 = \frac{3}{4}\,\rho_4(R_{\rm sh})\,\dot{R}_{\rm sh}^2.

   .. note::

      Some references omit the :math:`3/4` prefactor, which introduces only an
      order-unity correction. Triceratops retains the full factor.

   Substituting the self-similar ansatz :math:`R_{\rm sh} = A_{\rm sh}\,t^\lambda`
   and collecting terms yields the expression for :math:`\zeta` above.

----

Density Normalizations
-----------------------

Ejecta Density
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

To express :math:`A_1` in terms of observable parameters, we adopt a
broken power-law ejecta profile with inner slope :math:`\delta` and outer
slope :math:`n`, motivated by hydrodynamical simulations:

.. math::

   \rho(r,t) = K\,t^{-3}
   \begin{cases}
       v^{-\delta}, & v < v_t, \\
       v_t^{n-\delta}\,v^{-n}, & v \geq v_t.
   \end{cases}

.. admonition:: Key Result: Ejecta Normalization

   In terms of total ejecta mass :math:`M_{\rm ej}` and kinetic energy
   :math:`E_{\rm ej}`:

   .. math::

      v_t^2 =
      \frac{2(5-\delta)(n-5)}{(3-\delta)(n-3)}
      \frac{E_{\rm ej}}{M_{\rm ej}},

   .. math::

      K =
      \frac{1}{4\pi}
      \left(\frac{(3-\delta)(n-3)}{n-\delta}\right)
      \frac{M_{\rm ej}}{v_t^{3-\delta}}.

.. dropdown:: Derivation: Mass and Energy Integrals
   :icon: book-open

   Mass conservation requires

   .. math::

      M_{\rm ej}
      = 4\pi K\,v_t^{3-\delta}
        \frac{n-\delta}{(3-\delta)(n-3)}.

   Energy conservation gives

   .. math::

      E_{\rm ej}
      = 2\pi K\,v_t^{5-\delta}
        \frac{n-\delta}{(5-\delta)(n-5)}.

   Dividing the energy integral by the mass integral eliminates :math:`K`
   and yields :math:`v_t`; substituting back gives :math:`K`.

CSM Density
~~~~~~~~~~~

The CSM normalization :math:`A_4` may be specified directly or related to a
fiducial density via

.. math::

   \rho_{\rm CSM}(r) = \rho_0 \left(\frac{r}{r_0}\right)^{-s} = A_4\,r^{-s}.

**Wind-like CSM** (:math:`s = 2`)

For a steady progenitor wind, mass conservation fixes :math:`A_4`:

.. math::

   A_4 = \frac{\dot{M}}{4\pi v_w},

where :math:`\dot{M}` is the progenitor mass-loss rate and :math:`v_w` is the
wind velocity. This is the most widely used normalization for modeling CSM
around massive stars that underwent steady mass loss prior to explosion.

----

Two-Shock Self-Similar Structure
---------------------------------

The thin-shell model described above is useful because it gives an analytic
radius normalization and a fast estimate of the contact-discontinuity dynamics.
However, the original Chevalier solution\ :footcite:p:`chevalierSelfsimilarSolutionsInteraction1982` is not a literal
single-shell model. It resolves the shocked interaction region **into two
self-similar layers separated by a contact discontinuity**:

- **Region 2**: shocked ejecta, bounded by the reverse shock and the contact
  discontinuity.
- **Region 3**: shocked CSM, bounded by the contact discontinuity and the
  forward shock.

The two-shock solver implemented in Triceratops follows this more complete
structure. Instead of approximating the shocked region as a single thin shell,
it solves the self-similar hydrodynamic equations on both sides of the contact
discontinuity and determines the dimensionless constants

.. math::

   A,\qquad \frac{R_{\rm fs}}{R_c},\qquad \frac{R_{\rm rs}}{R_c},

where :math:`R_c` is the contact-discontinuity radius,
:math:`R_{\rm fs}` is the forward-shock radius, and
:math:`R_{\rm rs}` is the reverse-shock radius.

The full time-dependent kinematics then follow from

.. math::

   R_c(t)
   =
   \left[
      A\,\frac{A_1}{A_4}
   \right]^{1/(n-s)}
   t^{(n-3)/(n-s)},

where :math:`A_1` is the outer ejecta density normalization and
:math:`A_4` is the CSM density normalization. The forward and reverse shock
radii are fixed multiples of the contact radius:

.. math::

   R_{\rm fs}(t)
   =
   \left(\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{R_c}\right)R_c(t),
   \qquad
   R_{\rm rs}(t)
   =
   \left(\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{R_c}\right)R_c(t).

Since all three surfaces evolve with the same power-law index, their velocities
are

.. math::

   v_c = m\,\frac{R_c}{t},
   \qquad
   v_{\rm fs} = m\,\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{t},
   \qquad
   v_{\rm rs} = m\,\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{t},

where

.. math::

   m = \frac{n-3}{n-s}.

.. note::

   The original Chevalier paper uses the reciprocal exponent

   .. math::

      \Lambda = \frac{1}{m} = \frac{n-s}{n-3}.

   In the code, ``expansion_index`` refers to :math:`m`, while
   ``similarity_exponent`` refers to :math:`\Lambda`.


Similarity Variables
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The two-sided solver uses a common similarity form for both shocked regions.
On each side we define a local shock-normalized coordinate

.. math::

   x = \frac{r}{R_{\rm sh}(t)},

where :math:`R_{\rm sh}=R_{\rm fs}` for the outer shocked CSM solution and
:math:`R_{\rm sh}=R_{\rm rs}` for the inner shocked ejecta solution. The shock
is always located at :math:`x=1`.

The velocity, density, and pressure are written as

.. math::

   u(r,t) = \frac{r}{t}U(x),

.. math::

   \rho(r,t) = \rho_0\,t^a r^b \Omega(x),

.. math::

   p(r,t) = \rho_0\,t^{a-2}r^{b+2}P(x).

The powers :math:`a` and :math:`b` depend on which side is being solved.

For the **outer shocked CSM**,

.. math::

   \rho_{\rm CSM} = A_4 r^{-s},
   \qquad
   a=0,
   \qquad
   b=-s,
   \qquad
   \rho_0=A_4.

For the **inner shocked ejecta**,

.. math::

   \rho_{\rm ej}
   =
   A_1 t^{n-3}r^{-n},
   \qquad
   a=n-3,
   \qquad
   b=-n,
   \qquad
   \rho_0=A_1.

The dimensionless sound speed is

.. math::

   C^2 = \frac{\gamma P}{\Omega}.

The contact discontinuity is identified by the condition that the fluid velocity
matches the contact velocity. Since

.. math::

   v_c = m\,\frac{R_c}{t},

and :math:`u=(r/t)U`, the contact condition is

.. math::

   U \rightarrow m.

This condition is approached from opposite sides in the two integrations. In
the outer shocked CSM, the integration starts at the forward shock and proceeds
inward until :math:`U \rightarrow m`. In the inner shocked ejecta, the
integration starts at the reverse shock and proceeds outward until
:math:`U \rightarrow m`.


Self-Similar ODE System
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Substituting the similarity ansatz into the spherical Euler equations gives a
first-order ODE system for :math:`U(x)`, :math:`\Omega(x)`, and :math:`P(x)`.
The solver uses the same algebraic form for both sides; only the powers
:math:`a` and :math:`b` change.

Define

.. math::

   D = x(U-m).

Then the dimensionless equations are

.. math::

   D\frac{\Omega'}{\Omega}
   +
   xU'
   +
   a + (b+3)U
   =
   0,

.. math::

   U^2-U + D U'
   =
   -\frac{(b+2)P+xP'}{\Omega},

and

.. math::

   D\left(
      \frac{P'}{P}
      -
      \gamma\frac{\Omega'}{\Omega}
   \right)
   +
   (a-2-\gamma a)
   +
   (b+2-\gamma b)U
   =
   0.

Here primes denote derivatives with respect to :math:`x`.

For numerical stability, these equations are solved as a linear system for
:math:`U'` and :math:`P'`, after which :math:`\Omega'` is recovered from the
continuity equation. The matrix form is

.. math::

   \begin{pmatrix}
      D & x/\Omega \\
      \gamma P x & D
   \end{pmatrix}
   \begin{pmatrix}
      U' \\
      P'
   \end{pmatrix}
   =
   \begin{pmatrix}
      U-U^2-\dfrac{(b+2)P}{\Omega} \\
      -P\left[
         E+\gamma\left(a+(b+3)U\right)
      \right]
   \end{pmatrix},

where

.. math::

   E =
   (a-2-\gamma a)
   +
   (b+2-\gamma b)U.

The density derivative is then

.. math::

   \Omega'
   =
   \Omega\,
   \frac{
      -xU'-a-(b+3)U
   }{D}.

.. dropdown:: Derivation: Similarity Reduction of the Euler Equations
   :icon: book-open

   The starting point is the spherical Euler system

   .. math::

      \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}
      +
      \frac{1}{r^2}
      \frac{\partial}{\partial r}
      \left(r^2\rho u\right)
      =
      0,

   .. math::

      \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}
      +
      u\frac{\partial u}{\partial r}
      +
      \frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial p}{\partial r}
      =
      0,

   and the adiabatic entropy equation

   .. math::

      \left(
         \frac{\partial}{\partial t}
         +
         u\frac{\partial}{\partial r}
      \right)
      \left(
         p\rho^{-\gamma}
      \right)
      =
      0.

   Let

   .. math::

      x = \frac{r}{R_{\rm sh}(t)},
      \qquad
      R_{\rm sh}(t)\propto t^m.

   At fixed :math:`r`,

   .. math::

      \left.\frac{\partial x}{\partial t}\right|_r
      =
      -\frac{mx}{t},

   while

   .. math::

      \frac{\partial x}{\partial r}
      =
      \frac{x}{r}.

   With

   .. math::

      u = \frac{r}{t}U(x),
      \qquad
      \rho = \rho_0 t^a r^b \Omega(x),
      \qquad
      p = \rho_0 t^{a-2}r^{b+2}P(x),

   the continuity equation becomes

   .. math::

      x(U-m)\frac{\Omega'}{\Omega}
      +
      xU'
      +
      a+(b+3)U
      =
      0.

   The momentum equation becomes

   .. math::

      U^2-U+x(U-m)U'
      =
      -\frac{(b+2)P+xP'}{\Omega}.

   Finally, applying the Lagrangian derivative to
   :math:`p\rho^{-\gamma}` gives

   .. math::

      x(U-m)
      \left(
         \frac{P'}{P}
         -
         \gamma\frac{\Omega'}{\Omega}
      \right)
      +
      (a-2-\gamma a)
      +
      (b+2-\gamma b)U
      =
      0.

   These are the equations solved by
   :func:`~triceratops.dynamics.shocks.chevalier.compute_self_similar_functions`.


Shock Boundary Conditions
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The integrations begin from the immediate post-shock states. We assume strong,
cold shocks with adiabatic index :math:`\gamma`.

Outer Forward Shock
^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^

The outer shock propagates into stationary CSM. Its velocity is

.. math::

   V_{\rm fs} = m\,\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{t}.

For a strong shock into cold stationary material, the immediate downstream
state is

.. math::

   u_1 = \frac{2}{\gamma+1}V_{\rm fs},

.. math::

   \rho_1 =
   \frac{\gamma+1}{\gamma-1}\rho_{\rm CSM}(R_{\rm fs}),

.. math::

   p_1 =
   \frac{2}{\gamma+1}
   \rho_{\rm CSM}(R_{\rm fs})V_{\rm fs}^2.

In dimensionless form this gives

.. math::

   U_1 = \frac{2m}{\gamma+1},

.. math::

   \Omega_1 = \frac{\gamma+1}{\gamma-1},

.. math::

   P_1 = \frac{2m^2}{\gamma+1}.

The outer integration starts at :math:`x=1` and proceeds inward toward the
contact discontinuity.

Inner Reverse Shock
^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^

The reverse shock propagates into homologously expanding ejecta. The upstream
ejecta velocity at the reverse shock is

.. math::

   u_{\rm ej}(R_{\rm rs},t) = \frac{R_{\rm rs}}{t},

while the reverse-shock speed is

.. math::

   V_{\rm rs} = m\,\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{t}.

The upstream ejecta therefore overtakes the reverse shock with relative speed

.. math::

   v_{\rm rel}
   =
   \frac{R_{\rm rs}}{t}-V_{\rm rs}
   =
   (1-m)\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{t}.

The immediate downstream state is

.. math::

   \rho_2 =
   \frac{\gamma+1}{\gamma-1}\rho_{\rm ej}(R_{\rm rs},t),

.. math::

   p_2 =
   \frac{2}{\gamma+1}
   \rho_{\rm ej}(R_{\rm rs},t)v_{\rm rel}^2,

and

.. math::

   u_2 =
   V_{\rm rs}
   +
   \frac{\gamma-1}{\gamma+1}v_{\rm rel}.

In dimensionless form,

.. math::

   U_2
   =
   m
   +
   \frac{\gamma-1}{\gamma+1}(1-m),

.. math::

   \Omega_2
   =
   \frac{\gamma+1}{\gamma-1},

.. math::

   P_2
   =
   \frac{2(1-m)^2}{\gamma+1}.

For :math:`\gamma=5/3`, these reduce to the boundary conditions used by
:footcite:t:`chevalierSelfsimilarSolutionsInteraction1982`,

.. math::

   U_2 = \frac{1}{4}\left(1+3m\right),

.. math::

   \Omega_2 = 4,

.. math::

   P_2 = \frac{3}{4}(1-m)^2.

The inner integration starts at :math:`x=1` and proceeds outward toward the
contact discontinuity.

Determining the Shock Radii
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Each side is solved in its own local shock-normalized coordinate
:math:`x=r/R_{\rm sh}`. The contact discontinuity is located by the event

.. math::

   U(x_c) = m.

Since the shock is at :math:`x=1`, the contact location gives the shock radius
relative to the contact radius:

.. math::

   \frac{R_{\rm sh}}{R_c} = \frac{1}{x_c}.

Therefore,

.. math::

   \frac{R_{\rm fs}}{R_c}
   =
   \frac{1}{x_{c,{\rm out}}},
   \qquad
   \frac{R_{\rm rs}}{R_c}
   =
   \frac{1}{x_{c,{\rm in}}}.

In practice, the integration stops slightly before the formal contact
singularity by using

.. math::

   U = m \mp \epsilon,

with the sign chosen according to which side is being integrated. This avoids
evaluating exactly at the contact, where density or temperature may be singular
even though pressure remains finite.


Pressure Matching and the Constant :math:`A`
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The inner and outer solutions are joined by enforcing pressure continuity at the
contact discontinuity. Define

.. math::

   C_1
   =
   \left(\frac{p_c}{p_1}\right)_{\rm outer},

and

.. math::

   C_2
   =
   \left(\frac{p_c}{p_2}\right)_{\rm inner},

where :math:`p_1` and :math:`p_2` are the immediate post-forward-shock and
post-reverse-shock pressures, respectively.

Because the physical pressure contains an explicit radial factor,

.. math::

   p \propto r^{b+2}P(x),

the contact-to-shock pressure ratios are

.. math::

   C_1
   =
   x_{c,{\rm out}}^{2-s}
   \frac{P(x_{c,{\rm out}})}{P_1},

for the outer shocked CSM, and

.. math::

   C_2
   =
   x_{c,{\rm in}}^{2-n}
   \frac{P(x_{c,{\rm in}})}{P_2},

for the inner shocked ejecta.

Pressure continuity requires

.. math::

   C_1 p_1 = C_2 p_2.

Solving this condition for the radius normalization gives

.. math::

   A
   =
   \frac{C_2}{C_1}
   \left(\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{R_c}\right)^{2-n}
   \left(\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{R_c}\right)^{s-2}
   \left(\frac{1-m}{m}\right)^2.

Equivalently, since

.. math::

   \frac{1-m}{m} = \frac{3-s}{n-3},

we can write

.. math::

   A
   =
   \frac{C_2}{C_1}
   \left(\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{R_c}\right)^{2-n}
   \left(\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{R_c}\right)^{s-2}
   \left(\frac{3-s}{n-3}\right)^2.

This is the dimensionless constant used in the two-shock solver to normalize
the contact radius.

.. dropdown:: Derivation: Pressure Matching and Radius Normalization
   :icon: book-open

   The immediate post-forward-shock pressure is

   .. math::

      p_1
      =
      \frac{2}{\gamma+1}
      A_4 R_{\rm fs}^{-s}
      \left(
         m\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{t}
      \right)^2.

   The immediate post-reverse-shock pressure is

   .. math::

      p_2
      =
      \frac{2}{\gamma+1}
      A_1 t^{n-3}R_{\rm rs}^{-n}
      \left[
         (1-m)\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{t}
      \right]^2.

   Therefore,

   .. math::

      \frac{p_2}{p_1}
      =
      \frac{A_1}{A_4}
      t^{n-3}
      R_{\rm rs}^{2-n}
      R_{\rm fs}^{s-2}
      \left(\frac{1-m}{m}\right)^2.

   Writing

   .. math::

      R_{\rm fs}
      =
      \left(\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{R_c}\right)R_c,
      \qquad
      R_{\rm rs}
      =
      \left(\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{R_c}\right)R_c,

   gives

   .. math::

      \frac{p_2}{p_1}
      =
      \frac{A_1}{A_4}
      t^{n-3}
      R_c^{s-n}
      \left(\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{R_c}\right)^{2-n}
      \left(\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{R_c}\right)^{s-2}
      \left(\frac{1-m}{m}\right)^2.

   The contact radius is defined by

   .. math::

      R_c^{n-s}
      =
      A\,\frac{A_1}{A_4}t^{n-3}.

   Hence

   .. math::

      \frac{A_1}{A_4}t^{n-3}R_c^{s-n}
      =
      \frac{1}{A}.

   Therefore,

   .. math::

      \frac{p_2}{p_1}
      =
      \frac{1}{A}
      \left(\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{R_c}\right)^{2-n}
      \left(\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{R_c}\right)^{s-2}
      \left(\frac{1-m}{m}\right)^2.

   Pressure continuity at the contact gives

   .. math::

      C_1 p_1 = C_2 p_2,

   or

   .. math::

      \frac{p_2}{p_1} = \frac{C_1}{C_2}.

   Combining the last two expressions yields

   .. math::

      A
      =
      \frac{C_2}{C_1}
      \left(\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{R_c}\right)^{2-n}
      \left(\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{R_c}\right)^{s-2}
      \left(\frac{1-m}{m}\right)^2.
