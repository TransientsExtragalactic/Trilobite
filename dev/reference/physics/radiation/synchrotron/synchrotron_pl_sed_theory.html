Methods: Single-Zone Synchrotron SEDs#

See also

  • Synchrotron Radiation Theory for a broad discussion of the relevant synchrotron theory used in this theory module.

  • Theory Note: Electron Populations for a discussion of synchrotron cooling and populations. Some of this theory is relevant for the discussion in this note.

This document is intended to develop the theory behind Trilobite’ implementation of single-zone synchrotron SEDs. This is a critical task on the basis that the literature, spanning some 40 years at this point, is highly fractured in its methodology concerning these SEDs and their construction (see e.g. Chevalier[1], Chevalier and Fransson[2], Granot and Sari[3], Gao et al.[4], Sfaradi et al.[5], and references therein). Our goal in this presentation is to (a) provide a background to users who are not familiar with the details of this theory and, more importantly, (b) to establish our methodology in as robust a manner as possible ensuring that Trilobite remains extensible, reproducible, and accurate.

Overview#

The theory of single-zone synchrotron SEDs as described in this theory note is highly non-trivial and must be done with exceeding care. In the subsections below, we will describe in detail each element of SED construction; however, for those familiar with the literature, it should be noted that we follow the formulation of Granot and Sari[3] to construct our SEDs and to describe the various power-law components. Because we include an additional break frequency (\(\nu_{\rm max}\)) a few additional scenarios are considered here which are not therein mentioned.

Because Granot and Sari[3] use a methodology for normalization which is not fully generalizable without numerical quadrature, we choose instead to follow the approximations described in Sari et al.[6] and used throughout the literature (see e.g. Sfaradi et al.[5]).

Important

Throughout this note, for the sake of clarity, we restrict ourself to the comoving frame of the emitting region. The implementations of these SEDs in the code use the appropriate Lorentz transformations to ensure that the SEDs are correctly computed in the observer frame. However, the theory is more straightforwardly described in the comoving frame and so we adopt this convention here.

Foundational Assumptions#

In developing the theory here described, a number of assumptions are made. Of these, a few are foundational to the model and should not, under any circumstances, be violated. These include:

  • The emission region is a single zone, meaning that electrons emitting synchrotron are all well described by the same physical parameters (magnetic field strength, electron distribution, etc.). This need not imply that the source is a point-source; only that its combined emission may be described as a single, effective radiation zone.

  • The injected electron distribution is a power law in Lorentz factor, with a minimum Lorentz factor \(\gamma_m\), a power-law index \(p\), and (optionally) a maximum Lorentz factor \(\gamma_M\).

Parameters, Hyperparameters, and Physical Quantities#

This document contains a great deal of mathematical notation describing the various SEDs and their construction. It is therefore worthwhile to provide a bird’s-eye view of the various types of quantities used herein.

At it’s core, an SED is a function \(F(\nu, F_{\rm norm}, \boldsymbol{\nu}_{\rm brk}, \boldsymbol{\Theta})\) which provides the flux density \(F_\nu\) at a given frequency \(\nu\) given a set of break frequencies \(\boldsymbol{\nu}_{\rm brk} = (\nu_1, \nu_2, \ldots)\), a normalization, and a set of hyper-parameters \(\boldsymbol{\Theta} = (\theta_1, \theta_2, \ldots)\).

  • The break frequencies are the critical frequencies which define the transitions between different power-law segments in the SED. These are, in general, functions of the underlying physical parameters of the system such as magnetic field strength, electron distribution properties, etc.

    Important

    Throughout this document, we refer to the observer-frame quantities without a prime and to the rest-frame quantities with a prime.

  • The hyper-parameters are parameters which define the shape of the SED but are not directly related to the physical parameters of the system. Examples include the electron power-law index \(p\) and the smoothness parameters \(s_{(i,j)}\) which define the sharpness of transitions between power-law segments. This also includes parameters such as the pitch angle \(\alpha\), the filling factor \(f\), etc. These may also be functions of the underlying physical parameters.

In addition to the break frequencies and hyper-parameters, there may be internal parameters which are self-consistently determined from the other parameters. An example of this is the self-absorption frequency \(\nu_a\) which is, in general, a function of the other break frequencies and hyper-parameters.

Single-Zone SED Structure#

As described above, the single-zone SED is a broken power law with a number of break frequencies and a normalization. The exact structure of the SED depends on the relative ordering of the break frequencies and the value of the hyper-parameters. In keeping with the formalism of Granot and Sari[3], we adopt a mathematical formalism in which the SED is described as the product of a normalized power-law segment and a series of multiplicative factors which describe the transitions between power-law segments.

SED Surgery#

Each SED is anchored by a single power-law segment (SPLS) determined by the dominant break frequency and the relevant hyper-parameters. This takes the form

\[F_\nu = F_{\rm pk} \left(\frac{\nu}{\nu_{\rm brk}}\right)^{\alpha_0},\]

where \(\nu_{\rm brk}\) is the dominant break frequency and \(\alpha_0\) is the spectral slope of the anchored segment.

Additional breaks are introduced through SED Surgery, in which multiplicative, scale-free modification factors are applied at the relevant break frequencies.

For a break at \(\nu_i\), we introduce the kernel

\[\tilde f(x) = \left[ 1 + x^{(a_{\rm right} - a_{\rm left})/s} \right]^s, \qquad x = \frac{\nu}{\nu_i},\]

where:

  • \(a_{\rm left}\) is the spectral slope for \(\nu < \nu_i\),

  • \(a_{\rm right}\) is the spectral slope for \(\nu > \nu_i\),

  • \(s\) is the smoothness parameter.

This kernel enforces a transition from \(a_{\rm left}\) on the left to \(a_{\rm right}\) on the right, with total slope change

\[\Delta\alpha = a_{\rm right} - a_{\rm left}.\]

The magnitude \(|s|\) controls the sharpness of the break, with smaller values approaching the sharp broken power-law limit.

The sign of \(s\) must be determined based on the curvature of the transition:

  • \(s > 0\) produces a concave-down transition.

  • \(s < 0\) produces a concave-up transition.

The full SED is therefore

\[F_\nu = F_{\rm pk} \left(\frac{\nu}{\nu_{\rm brk}}\right)^{\alpha_0} \prod_i \tilde f_i\!\left(\frac{\nu}{\nu_i}\right),\]

and in the limit \(|s|\to 0\) this reduces exactly to the canonical broken power-law spectrum anchored at \(F_{\rm pk}\).

Break Frequencies#

Before proceeding to discuss the construction of various SEDs, it is necessary to describe the precise methodology with which we compute the various break frequencies used in the SEDs. In each of the following sections, we describe this methodology in detail.

The Injection Frequencies#

PARAMETER TYPE: Free Parameter

The injection frequencies are a function of the detailed microphysics of shock acceleration and must, in general, be prescribed by some downstream model of that physics. In the context of SED construction, the critical one should, in general, use whichever physical prescription is most appropriate to compute \(\gamma_{\rm min}\) and \(\gamma_{\rm max}\).

Once these Lorentz factors are know, the injection frequencies are fixed up to a factor for the treatment of pitch angle \(\alpha\) between the electron velocity and the magnetic field. As we have done elsewhere, we support both the isotropic pitch angle averaged case and the fixed pitch angle case. In each case, the (rest-frame) injection frequencies are as follows:

In the case of an isotropic pitch angle distribution, our convention for the pitch averaged break frequency is to use the average synchrotron frequency given by (1) from Synchrotron Radiation Theory. Thus,

(1)#\[\nu_m = \left<\nu_{\rm synch}(\gamma)\right>_{\sin \alpha} = \frac{3 q B}{2 \pi^2 m c}\left[\gamma_{\min}\right]^2,\]

In the same manner, the maximum injection frequency is

\[\nu_{\rm max} = \left<\nu_{\rm synch}(\gamma)\right>_{\sin \alpha} = \frac{3 q B}{2 \pi^2 m c}\left[\gamma_{\max}\right]^2.\]

In the case of a fixed pitch angle \(\alpha\), the injection frequencies are given by the standard characteristic synchrotron frequency from (1) from Synchrotron Radiation Theory:

(2)#\[\nu_m = \nu_{\rm char}(\gamma_{\min}) = \frac{3 q B \sin \alpha}{4 \pi m c}\left[\gamma_{\min}\right]^2,\]

and

\[\nu_{\rm max} = \nu_{\rm char}(\gamma_{\max}) = \frac{3 q B \sin \alpha}{4 \pi m c}\left[\gamma_{\max}\right]^2.\]

Hint

It is somewhat confusing that \(\nu_m\) is a free parameter, but \(\gamma_{\min}\) is a hyper-parameter. The difference between these two quantities is a factor of \(B\), the magnetic field strength. Generally, \(\gamma_{\rm min}\) (or \(\gamma_{\rm max}\)) is modeled a priori from shock acceleration theory, while \(B\) is determined from the overall energetics of the system and is therefore treated as a physical parameter. Because \(\nu_m\) depends on both, it is treated as a free parameter.

Convention Note

This particular quantity is subject to a wide range of conventions in the literature (see e.g. Granot and Sari[3], Gao et al.[4], Sfaradi et al.[5] DeMarchi et al.[7]). We have here taken the route of defining the injection frequencies in a manner most in keeping with the general theory of synchrotron radiation as described in Synchrotron Radiation Theory.

The Cooling Frequency#

PARAMETER TYPE: Free Parameter

Consider a population of electrons subject to a cooling process with a cooling rate \(\Lambda(\gamma)\). Electrons with energy \(E = m_e c^2 \gamma\) will then cool on a timescale (in the comoving frame)

\[t_{\rm cool}(\gamma) = \frac{E}{\Lambda(\gamma)} = \frac{m_e c^2 \gamma}{\Lambda(\gamma)}.\]

If, in order to cool significantly, the dynamical time must exceed the cooling time for a particular energy, we can define the cooling Lorentz factor \(\gamma_c\) as the Lorentz factor for which the cooling timescale equals the dynamical timescale \(t_{\rm dyn}\) of the system:

\[\boxed{ t_{\rm cool}(\gamma_c) = \frac{m_e c^2 \gamma_c}{\Lambda(\gamma_c)} = t_{\rm dyn}. }\]

This then implies

(3)#\[\boxed{ \gamma_c = \frac{m_e c^2}{\Lambda(\gamma_c) t_{\rm dyn}}. }\]

The corresponding cooling frequency is then given by (1) from Synchrotron Radiation Theory as

(4)#\[\boxed{ \nu_c = \frac{3 q B \sin \alpha}{4 \pi m c} \gamma_c^2 = \frac{3 q m_e c^3}{4\pi} \left( \frac{B \sin \alpha} {\left[\Lambda(\gamma_c)\right]^2\, t_{\rm dyn}^{\,2}} \right). }\]

The precise value of the cooling frequency should be determined from the dominant cooling process affecting the electron population. In most astrophysical synchrotron sources, the most important cooling processes are synchrotron cooling and inverse Compton cooling.

One can likewise consider the case where multiple cooling processes are acting simultaneously. In such a case, the total cooling rate is the sum of the individual cooling rates and the formalism above applies similarly. (4) is the homogeneous pitch-angle averaged cooling frequency; however, one may also use the pitch-averaged cooling frequency as

\[\boxed{ \nu_c = \left(\frac{1+z}{\delta}\right) \frac{3 q m_e c^3}{2 \pi^2} \left[\frac{B\sin\alpha}{\left[\Lambda(\gamma_c)\right]^2 t_{\rm dyn}^2}\right] }\]

Hint

In general, one selects a cooling mechanism (see Synchrotron Radiation Theory and Synchrotron Microphysics) and computes \(\gamma_c(t)\) as a function of time and provides that to the SED. Cooling is implemented in cooling.

The Absorption Frequency#

PARAMETER TYPE: Internal Parameter

The self-absorption frequency is a less trivial quantity to compute, as it depends on the radiative transfer properties of the source and is therefore dependent on the SED one is using. This creates a circular dependency which must be resolved by considering every possible SED configuration given a known \(\nu_m\) and \(\nu_c\), computing the value of \(\nu_a\) in each case and then checking for self-consistency with the assumed SED and its assumptions.

In the most rigorous sense, the absorption frequency \(\nu_a\) is determined by the condition that the optical depth to self-absorption equals unity:

\[\tau_{\nu_{a}} = \alpha_{\nu_a} L = 1,\]

The form of \(\alpha_\nu\) depends explicitly on the structure of the absorbing electron population (see Synchrotron Radiation Theory for details). One could, in principle, perform these computations in full detail; however, an alternative approach has been developed in the literature [8] which allows for approximate expressions for \(\nu_a\).

We assume, as was done in the development of the normalization approach, that the absorption at a particular frequency is dominated by a mono-energetic population of electrons. In such a case, the optically thick emission from the source should be well approximated by a blackbody with brightness temperature \(kT_{b} = \gamma_\nu m_e c^2\), where \(\gamma_\nu\) is the Lorentz factor corresponding to the dominant absorbing population of electrons:

\[\gamma_\nu = {\rm max}\left(\gamma_a, {\rm min}\left(\gamma_c,\gamma_m\right)\right).\]

This corresponds to a source function \(S_\nu = 2\left[\nu_a\right]^2 m_e \gamma_\nu\). The corresponding flux \(F_\nu\) should then be

\[F_\nu = 2\left[\nu_a\right]^2 m_e \gamma_\nu \frac{A}{D_A^2},\]

where \(A\) is the effective radiating area of the source. Equating this to the optically thin flux from the normalized SED at \(\nu_a\) then allows one to solve for \(\nu_a\). In practice, we instead parameterize this flux density in terms of the effective angular radiating area \(\Omega = A/D_A^2\) so that the distance does not need to be provided as a hyper-parameter.

Important

For code users, it is important to note that while the inclusion of \(\nu_a\) would seem to be similar in complexity to the other breaks, using an SSA enabled SED REQUIRES the user to provide some elements of the underlying geometry of the source (i.e., the effective area and volume) in order to compute \(\nu_a\) in each dependent scenario.

Thus, an SSA SED function \(F_{\rm \nu}(\nu; \nu_m,\nu_c,\nu_a,\ldots)\) should be thought of more properly as

\[F_{\rm \nu}(\nu; \nu_m,\nu_c,A,V,\ldots),\]

where now \(\nu_a = f(\nu_m,\nu_c,A,V,\ldots)\) is computed internally.

Computing The Self-Absorption Frequency#

In general, we assume that, at \(\nu_a\) (an unknown break), the flux density is given by the blackbody approximation described above:

\[F_\nu = 2\left[\nu_a\right]^2 m_e \gamma_\nu \Omega = F_{\nu, \rm thin}(\nu_a),\]

where \(F_{\nu, \rm thin}(\nu_a)\) is the flux density at \(\nu_a\) computed from the optically thin SED, and \(\gamma_{\nu}\) is the Lorentz factor of the dominant absorbing electrons:

\[\gamma_\nu = {\rm max}\left(\gamma_a, {\rm min}\left(\gamma_c,\gamma_m\right)\right).\]

This defines an implicit equation for \(\nu_a\) which may be solved algebraically (or numerically if necessary). Thus, for any set of break frequencies and hyper-parameters, one may compute \(\nu_a\) by solving the equation and then construct the SED using the computed frequency.

The Single Power-Law Segment (SPLS)#

Because various broadband SEDs have segments with the same SPL slopes, we begin by listing the different possible SPL segments that can arise in synchrotron SEDs from power-law electron distributions. This follows the naming convention of Granot and Sari[3].

SPL A occurs in the optically thick regime below the self-absorption frequency \(\nu_a\), but above the minimum electron frequency \(\nu_m\). In this regime, the SED is dominated by self-absorbed synchrotron emission from the full power-law distribution of electrons. The resulting SED is derived in most references in synchrotron emission, (e.g. Rybicki and Lightman[9], Chapter 6; Pacholczyk[10]).

SPL B occurs in the optically thick regime below both the self-absorption frequency \(\nu_a\) and the minimum electron frequency \(\nu_m\). In this regime, the SED is dominated by self-absorbed synchrotron emission from the low-energy tail of the single-electron SED. This results in a characteristic spectral slope of \(F_\nu \propto \nu^{2}\).

More precisely, the absorption coefficient to synchrotron self-abortion is ((3) from Synchrotron Radiation Theory):

\[\alpha_\nu = -\frac{1}{8\pi m_e \nu^2} \int_{\gamma_{\min}}^{\gamma_{\max}} P(\nu,\gamma)\, \gamma^2 \frac{\partial}{\partial\gamma} \left[ \frac{1}{\gamma^2} \frac{dN}{d\gamma} \right] d\gamma.\]

In the low-frequency limit, the integral is dominated by the lowest energy electrons in the population and the frequency is in the low-frequency tail of those single-electron SEDs. In that case, the entire integral term scales as \(\nu^{1/3}\) and the absorption coefficient scales as \(\alpha_\nu \propto \nu^{-5/3}\). The emissivity in this case scales as \(j_\nu \propto \nu^{1/3}\) as well. The source function is then \(S_\nu = j_\nu/\alpha_\nu \propto \nu^{2}\), leading to the characteristic SPL B slope.

SPL C is a special case first identified in Granot et al.[11] in the context of GRB afterglows. It arises in scenarios with fast cooling such that the post-shock electrons are able to cool very rapidly compared to the dynamical timescale. This leads to a stratified structure in the source, with a thin layer of uncooled electrons near the shock front and a larger volume of cooled electrons behind them. In this case, the self-absorption is dominated by the cooler, downstream electrons, leading to a modified self-absorption regime with a characteristic slope of \(F_\nu \propto \nu^{11/8}\).

To see a derivation of this result, see the theory note: Theory Note: Stratified Synchrotron Absorption.

SPL D occurs in the optically thin regime below the minimum electron frequency \(\nu_m\). In this regime, the SED is dominated by synchrotron emission from the low-energy tail of the single-electron SED. This results in a characteristic spectral slope of \(F_\nu \propto \nu^{1/3}\).

SPL E has the same slope as SPL D, but occurs in the optically thin regime between the cooling frequency \(\nu_c\) and the SSA frequency \(\nu_a\). In this regime, the SED is dominated by synchrotron emission from the low-energy tail of the single-electron SED, similar to SPL D.

SPL E occurs in the optically thin regime above the cooling frequency \(\nu_c\) but below the minimum electron frequency \(\nu_m\). In this regime, the SED is dominated by synchrotron emission from the cooled portion of the electron distribution. Because this population has a characteristic electron index of \(p=2\), the emissivity scales as \(j_\nu \propto \nu^{-1/2}\).

SPL E occurs in the optically thin regime above the minimum electron frequency \(\nu_m\) but below the cooling frequency \(\nu_c\) (if present). In this regime, the SED is dominated by synchrotron emission from the full power-law distribution of electrons that have not yet cooled. This results in a characteristic spectral slope of \(F_\nu \propto \nu^{-(p-1)/2}\).

SPL H occurs in the optically thin regime above the cooling frequency \(\nu_c\). In this regime, the SED is dominated by synchrotron emission from the cooled portion of the electron distribution. This results in a characteristic spectral slope of \(F_\nu \propto \nu^{-p/2}\).

SPL I occurs in the extreme high-frequency regime above the maximum electron frequency \(\nu_{\max}\). In this regime, the SED is dominated by the exponential cutoff in the single-electron SEDs of the highest energy electrons. This results in a characteristic spectral slope of \(F_\nu \propto \nu^{1/2} \exp(-\nu/\nu_{\rm char})\).

In addition to each of the SPLs described above and the corresponding scale-free transition kernels, SEDs which have a high frequency cutoff \(\nu_{\rm max}\) also have an additional break. In this regime, the SED transitions from any of the optically thin segments (SPL F, SPL G, or SPL H) to the exponential cutoff segment (SPL I). Because the exponential cutoff is not a power law, we do not provide SBPL representations for these breaks, but instead provide exponential cutoff functions. In the smoothed case described above, we instead need a scale-free exponential cutoff function which does not interfere with the normalization of the SED at lower frequencies. We therefore define:

\[\begin{split}\tilde{\Phi}(\nu,\nu_{\max}) = \begin{cases} 1, & \nu < \nu_{\max} \\ \left(\frac{\nu}{\nu_{\max}}\right)^{1/2} \exp\left(1 -\frac{\nu}{\nu_{\max}}\right), & \nu \geq \nu_{\max}. \end{cases}\end{split}\]

SED Normalization#

We have, at this stage, fully developed the framework through which synchrotron SEDs are constructed it is worth discussing how one connects these SEDs to physical parameters. This is generically not possible without some concession of additional information because the SEDs themselves are scale-free. In other words, given a set of break frequencies and a normalization flux density, there are an infinite number of physical configurations which can produce the same observed SED. Therefore, to connect the SEDs to physical parameters, we must adopt a normalization / closure.

The most common such closure is that of equipartition between the energy in relativistic electrons and magnetic fields. This approach is widely used in the literature (see, e.g., DeMarchi et al.[7], Margutti et al.[12], Chevalier[13], Chevalier[14], among others) and is often justified on the grounds that it minimizes the total energy budget of the system. However, it is important to note that this is an assumption rather than a physical requirement. In other words, there is no guarantee that the system will actually be in equipartition, and indeed there is evidence from some systems that it is not.

In Trilobite, we implement the SEDs in a scale-free manner such that the user may select any normalization scheme they wish. This is achieved by separating the calculation of the break frequencies and spectral shapes from the normalization itself. The user may then implement any desired closure scheme to connect the SEDs to physical parameters. For example, one may choose to implement equipartition, or one may choose to implement a different closure based on, e.g., observations or theoretical considerations.

For convenience, we do provide a normalization scheme implemented directly in the SED classes of trilobite.radiation.synchrotron.SEDs which is based on Equipartition and is described here.

Convention Note

We here adopt a normalization scheme based on the approach of Sari et al.[15] and used in Sfaradi et al.[5], Sari et al.[6], Duran et al.[8], Beniamini et al.[16] among others. This is different from the approach used in Granot and Sari[3] which was an insufficiently general scheme to suite a modular codebase such as Trilobite.

The Normalizing Flux and Frequency#

While seemingly a simple undertaking, the normalization of the SEDs is a complex element of the theory. This is, in part, because various approaches have been used in the literature ranging from exact calculations using numerical quadrature to a variety of approximate schemes. In our case, we select a scheme based off of that described in Sari et al.[15], which is an approximate scheme based off the notion that, at an (optically thin) break frequency \(\nu_{\rm brk}\), the synchrotron emission is dominated a single set of electrons with lorentz factors \(\gamma_{\rm brk}\) and number density \(n_e(\gamma_{\rm brk})\). One may, therefore, use this approximation to estimate the normalization to within a factor of order unity.

To be more precise, consider a population of electrons with a distribution of Lorentz factors \(dN/d\gamma(\gamma)\). The emissivity of synchrotron emission for such a population (see Synchrotron Radiation Theory) is

\[j_\nu = \frac{\sqrt{3} q^3 B \sin \alpha}{4\pi mc^2} \int_{\gamma_{\rm min}}^{\gamma_{\rm max}} \frac{dN}{d\gamma} F\left( \frac{\nu}{\nu_c(\gamma)} \right) d\gamma,\]

where \(q\) is the electron charge, \(B\) is the magnetic field strength, \(\gamma_{\rm min}\) and \(\gamma_{\rm max}\) are the bounding Lorentz factors for the population, and \(F\) is the synchrotron spectrum. \(\nu_c(\gamma)\) is the critical frequency function

\[\nu_c(\gamma) = \frac{3q}{4\pi m c} B \sin \alpha \gamma^2 = c_{1,\gamma} (B \sin \alpha) \gamma^2,\]

where \(c_{1,\gamma}\) is as defined in Synchrotron Radiation Theory.

Letting \(x(\gamma)\equiv \nu/\nu_c(\gamma)\), we have \(dx/d\gamma = -2x/\gamma\) and thus \(d\gamma = -(\gamma/2x)\,dx\). Since \(F(x)\) is sharply peaked near \(x\sim\mathcal{O}(1)\), the integral is dominated by \(\gamma=\gamma_\nu\) satisfying \(\nu\sim\nu_c(\gamma_\nu)\), and the remaining kernel integral contributes only an order-unity constant. Therefore,

\[j_\nu \approx \frac{\sqrt{3} q^3 B \sin \alpha}{4\pi m_e c^2}\,\left.\frac{dN}{d\gamma}\right|_{\gamma=\gamma_\nu}\,\gamma_\nu.\]

Note

We here, as in Sari et al.[15], suppress an order-unity factor stemming from integration of the kernel. Furthermore, in that paper, the value \(\frac{dN}{d\gamma}(\gamma_\nu) \gamma_\nu\) is approximated as an effective number density of electrons emitting at frequency \(\nu\). While this is a useful interpretation, we choose to retain the explicit dependence on the electron distribution function for clarity.

Assuming an effective emitting volume V at a luminosity distance \(D_L\),

\[F_\nu \approx \frac{V}{D_L^2} j_\nu.\]

Hint

A common approach in the literature which can be used, but is not required is to assume a spherical emitting region of radius \(R\) and a filling factor \(f\), such that \(V = f (4\pi R^3/3)\).

This is therefore the basis of our normalization scheme with a number of caveats:

  1. The approximation made above is only really sensible when used to describe the emissivity of the maximal population of electrons. We therefore always anchor our normalization to the peak emission frequency; however,

  2. The presence of absorption may obscure the optically thin peak. To correct for this, we use the known shape of the corresponding SED and the relevant break frequencies to “extrapolate” the optically thin peak down to the absorption break and then back along the correct PLS to correct the normalization for absorption effects.

To be more precise about which population of electrons we use to normalize the SED, we note that the determination of the correct population of electrons is determined by the cooling regime, with fast-cooling and slow-cooling scenarios corresponding to populations peaking at \(\nu_c\) and \(\nu_m\), respectively. In the tabs below, we describe the normalization for each case:

Important

For clarity of language, we emphasize a DIFFERENCE between the normalization frequency (i.e., the frequency at which we anchor the normalization of the SED) and the peak emission frequency (i.e., the frequency at which the SED peaks).

In SEDs, we NEVER refer directly to the normalization frequency. This is an internal conceptualization used to tie microphysics and dynamics to SED normalization. When parameterizing each SED, we normalize using the peak emission frequency which is calculated from the normalization frequency and power-law slopes of the SED.

In the case of a fast-cooling electron population, the flux of electrons below the injection frequency \(\nu_m\) produces a steady-state electron distribution which, by our conventions, (see Theory Note: Electron Populations) is

\[\begin{split}\frac{dN}{d\gamma} = K_0 \begin{cases} 0, & \gamma < \gamma_c \\ \left(\frac{\gamma}{\gamma_{\rm m}}\right)^{-2}, & \gamma_c \le \gamma < \gamma_{\rm m} \\ \left(\frac{\gamma}{\gamma_{\rm m}}\right)^{-(p+1)}, & \gamma_{\rm max} \ge \gamma \ge \gamma_{\rm m} \\ 0, & \gamma > \gamma_{\max} \end{cases},\end{split}\]

where \(K_0\) is the normalization constant of the electron distribution. One can obtain a normalization through many means, most commonly equipartition arguments or shock acceleration theory.

The corresponding normalizing flux and frequency are therefore \(F_{c,0}\) at \(\nu_c = \nu(\gamma_c)\). The population follows

\[\left.\frac{dN}{d\gamma}\right|_{\gamma=\gamma_c} = K_0 \left(\frac{\gamma_c}{\gamma_{\rm m}}\right)^{-2}.\]

Given that \(\gamma(\nu) \propto \nu^{1/2}\), we therefore have

\[\left.\frac{dN}{d\gamma}\right|_{\gamma=\gamma_\nu} = K_0 \left(\frac{\nu_m}{\nu_c}\right).\]

The corresponding emissivity at \(\nu_c\) is therefore

\[j_\nu \approx \frac{\sqrt{3} q^3 B \sin \alpha}{4\pi m_e c^2} K_0 \left(\frac{\nu_m}{\nu_c}\right)\,\gamma_{c}.\]

Collecting the constants, we have

(5)#\[\chi = \frac{\sqrt{3} q^3}{4\pi m_e c^2},\]

and

(6)#\[\chi_{\rm iso} = \frac{\sqrt{3} q^3}{2\pi^2 m_e c^2},\]

Normalization takes the form

(7)#\[F_{c,0} \approx \chi (B\sin\alpha) K_0 \left(\frac{\nu_m}{\nu_c}\right)\,\gamma_{c} \frac{V}{D_L^2},\]

or, equivalently, for the pitch-angle averaged case,

(8)#\[F_{c0,{\rm iso}} \approx \chi_{\rm iso} B K_0 \left(\frac{\nu_m}{\nu_c}\right)\,\gamma_{c} \frac{V}{D_L^2}.\]

In the case of a slow-cooling electron population, the distribution of electrons follows

\[\begin{split}\frac{dN}{d\gamma} = N_0 \begin{cases} 0, & \gamma < \gamma_{\rm m} \\ \left(\frac{\gamma}{\gamma_c}\right)^{-p}, & \gamma_{c} \ge \gamma \ge \gamma_{\rm m} \\ \left(\frac{\gamma}{\gamma_c}\right)^{-(p+1)}, & \gamma_{\rm max} \ge \gamma \ge \gamma_{c} \\ 0, & \gamma > \gamma_{\max} \end{cases},\end{split}\]

where \(N_0\) is the normalization constant of the electron distribution. As such

\[\left.\frac{dN}{d\gamma}\right|_{\gamma=\gamma_\nu} = N_0 \left(\frac{\gamma_\nu}{\gamma_c}\right)^{-p}.\]

so, at the normalizing frequency \(\nu_m = \nu(\gamma_{\min})\), we have

\[\left.\frac{dN}{d\gamma}\right|_{\gamma=\gamma_m} \gamma_{m} = N_0 \gamma_{\min}^{1-p} \gamma_c^{p}.\]

The emissivity is then

\[j_\nu \approx \frac{\sqrt{3} q^3 B \sin \alpha}{4\pi m_e c^2} N_0 \gamma_{\min}^{1-p} \gamma_c^{p}.\]

Collecting the constants, we have

\[\chi = \frac{\sqrt{3} q^3}{4\pi m_e c^2},\]

and

\[\chi_{\rm iso} = \frac{\sqrt{3} q^3}{2\pi^2 m_e c^2},\]

the normalizing flux at \(\nu_m\) is

(9)#\[F_{ms,0} \approx \chi (B\sin\alpha) N_0 \gamma_{\rm m}^{1-p} \gamma_c^{p} \frac{V}{D_L^2},\]

or, equivalently, for the pitch-angle averaged case,

(10)#\[F_{ms0,{\rm iso}} \approx \chi_{\rm iso} B N_0 \gamma_{\rm m}^{1-p} \frac{V}{D_L^2}.\]

In the case of a non-cooling electron population, the distribution of electrons follows

\[\begin{split}\frac{dN}{d\gamma} = N_0 \begin{cases} 0, & \gamma < \gamma_{\rm m} \\ \gamma^{-p}, & \gamma_{\max} \ge \gamma \ge \gamma_{\rm m} \\ 0, & \gamma > \gamma_{\max} \end{cases},\end{split}\]

where \(N_0\) is the normalization constant of the electron distribution. As such

\[\left.\frac{dN}{d\gamma}\right|_{\gamma=\gamma_\nu} = N_0 \gamma_\nu^{-p}.\]

so, at the normalizing frequency \(\nu_m = \nu(\gamma_{\min})\), we have

\[\left.\frac{dN}{d\gamma}\right|_{\gamma=\gamma_m} \gamma_{m} = N_0 \gamma_{\min}^{1-p}.\]

The emissivity is then

\[j_\nu \approx \frac{\sqrt{3} q^3 B \sin \alpha}{4\pi m_e c^2} N_0 \gamma_{\min}^{1-p}.\]

Collecting the constants, we have

\[\chi = \frac{\sqrt{3} q^3}{4\pi m_e c^2},\]

and

\[\chi_{\rm iso} = \frac{\sqrt{3} q^3}{2\pi^2 m_e c^2},\]

the normalizing flux at \(\nu_m\) is

(11)#\[F_{m,0} \approx \chi (B\sin\alpha) N_0 \gamma_{\rm m}^{1-p} \frac{V}{D_L^2},\]

or, equivalently, for the pitch-angle averaged case,

(12)#\[F_{m0,{\rm iso}} \approx \chi_{\rm iso} B N_0 \gamma_{\rm m}^{1-p} \frac{V}{D_L^2}.\]

Given a selected normalizing population (\(F_{c,0}\) at \(\nu_c\) for fast-cooling, \(F_{m,0}\) at \(\nu_m\) for non-cooling or \(F_{ms,0}\) at \(\nu_m\) for slow-cooling), one may then use equipartition to compute the relevant coefficients in the flux normalization expressions above. In the sections below, we’ll go through each of the SED cases and describe how to connect the normalization frequency to the peak emission frequency and thus the full SED normalization.

Power-Law Propagation of the Normalization#

The flux normalizations derived above (\(F_{m,0}\) at \(\nu_m\) in slow cooling and \(F_{c,0}\) at \(\nu_c\) in fast cooling) set the overall amplitude of the optically thin spectrum. However, in general these normalization frequencies do not coincide with the peak of the observed SED:

  • In the absence of self-absorption, the observed peak coincides with the optically thin peak.

  • When synchrotron self-absorption is important, the observed peak occurs at \(\nu_a\) and the optically thin peak may be obscured.

To connect the theoretical normalization (\(F_{m,0}\) or \(F_{c,0}\)) to the parameter \(F_{\rm pk}\) used to anchor the SED, we propagate the normalization across the relevant optically thin power-law segments.

In its most general form, if two frequencies \(\nu_1\) and \(\nu_2\) lie within the same optically thin power-law segment with slope \(\alpha\) (i.e. \(F_\nu \propto \nu^\alpha\)), then

\[F_\nu(\nu_2) = F_\nu(\nu_1)\left(\frac{\nu_2}{\nu_1}\right)^{\alpha}.\]

When multiple breaks lie between \(\nu_1\) and \(\nu_2\), propagation is performed piecewise across each segment:

\[F_\nu(\nu_2) = F_\nu(\nu_1)\prod_{j} \left(\frac{\nu_{j+1}}{\nu_{j}}\right)^{\alpha_j},\]

where \(\{\nu_j\}\) is the ordered list of intervening break frequencies (including endpoints) and \(\alpha_j\) is the spectral slope on the segment \((\nu_j,\nu_{j+1})\).

In practice, we use this propagation in two distinct ways:

  1. Optically thin peak (no SSA-dominated peak)

    If the spectrum is optically thin at its maximum, then the SED peak occurs at a known break frequency \(\nu_{\rm pk}\) (typically \(\nu_m\) or \(\nu_c\) depending on the cooling regime), and the peak flux is simply the corresponding normalization:

    \[\begin{split}(\nu_{\rm pk},F_{\rm pk}) = \begin{cases} (\nu_m, F_{ms,0}), & \text{slow cooling} \\ (\nu_c, F_{c,0}), & \text{fast cooling} \\ (\nu_m, F_{m,0}), & \text{no cooling} \end{cases}\end{split}\]

    In this case no propagation is required.

  2. SSA-dominated peak

    If self-absorption sets the observed peak, then \(\nu_{\rm pk}=\nu_a\) and the observed peak flux \(F_{\rm pk}=F_\nu(\nu_a)\) must be obtained by propagating the optically thin normalization up to \(\nu_a\) along the appropriate optically thin slopes, i.e.

    \[F_{\rm pk} = F_{\rm norm} \times \left[\text{optically thin propagation from }\nu_{\rm norm}\text{ to }\nu_a\right],\]

    where \((\nu_{\rm norm},F_{\rm norm})\) is \((\nu_m,F_{m,0})\) in slow cooling or \((\nu_c,F_{c,0})\) in fast cooling.

Note

This section describes our “standard” inversion, which is directly packaged into the synchrotron SEDs. This is by no means the only possible inversion, nor is it the only one which is useful. We have chosen this one on the basis that it is widely used in the literature and is a useful starting point for users. However, we encourage users to develop their own inversion schemes as needed for their particular applications.

In addition, some other inversion schemes are provided in the code with varying levels of applicability. The links below provide some examples of these other inversion schemes:

  • Theory Note: Synchrotron Cooling Closure provides a closure relation for which \(\gamma_c\) may be treated as a dependent variable and thus inferred from the SED. There are a number of caveats and restrictions to this approach which are described in the theory note.

The final theoretical topic which must be discussed in the context of our single-zone SED implementation is that of inversion. By inversion, we mean the process of taking an observed SED and inferring the underlying physical parameters which produced it. This is a non-trivial process which is often complicated by the fact that knowledge of the peak flux \(F_{\rm pk}\) and the peak frequency \(\nu_{\rm pk}\) alone is insufficient to break the degeneracies between the various physical parameters. In general, one must have some additional information (e.g., from dynamics or other observables) to break these degeneracies and infer the physical parameters.

It is therefore standard practice to invoke a closure relation to connect the SED parameters to physical parameters. As is the case for normalization, where one must invoke a closure relation to connect the SED to physical parameters, one must also invoke a closure relation to perform inversion.

Trilobite does not provide an exhaustive set of closure relations as, following our development philosophy, we prefer to allow for modeling flexibility rather than prescribing a particular set of assumptions. However, we do provide a few commonly used closure relations in the documentation, and we encourage users to implement their own closure relations as needed for their particular applications.

Hint

In many contexts, closure is not necessary: the user may construct a forward model which includes the relevant physical assumptions in the construction of the dynamical parameters used in normalization. The process of inference will then reveal the underlying physical parameters without the need for an explicit inversion of the SED. However, in some cases, it may be desirable to perform an explicit inversion of the SED, and in these cases, closure relations are required.

Closure Assumptions#

In what follows, we assume that the following observables are available for inversion:

  • The rest-frame break frequency \(\nu_{\rm brk}\) and its physical interpretation (e.g. \(\nu_m\) or \(\nu_c\)).

  • The rest-frame flux density at that break frequency, \(F_{\rm brk}\).

To close the system and permit inversion to physical parameters, we adopt the following physical assumptions:

The synchrotron emission arises from a population of relativistic electrons in either the fast- or slow-cooling regime (see Theory Note: Electron Populations). In both cases, the electron distribution may be written in separable form:

\[N(\gamma) = N_0 f(\gamma),\]

where \(f(\gamma)\) encodes the shape of the distribution and possesses a known first moment

\[M_\gamma^{(1)} \equiv \int \gamma f(\gamma)\, d\gamma.\]

We assume that fixed fractions of the post-shock internal energy density are deposited into relativistic electrons and magnetic fields:

\[u_e = \epsilon_e u_{\rm int}, \qquad u_B = \epsilon_B u_{\rm int} = \frac{B^2}{8\pi}.\]

Combining these relations yields the normalization closure

\[N_0 = \frac{\epsilon_e B^2} {8\pi \epsilon_B m_e c^2 M_\gamma^{(1)}} = \tilde{N_0} B^2,\]

where \(\tilde{N_0}\) is introduced to simplify the algebra.

We further assume that the emitting region is spherical with radius \(R\) and effective volume

\[V_{\rm eff} = \frac{4\pi}{3} R^3 f_V,\]

and angular size

\[\Omega = \frac{\pi R^2}{D_A^2} f_A.\]

For notational convenience, we introduce the constants

\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} \nu_{\rm synchrotron}(\gamma) &= c_{1,\rm ISO} B \gamma^2, \\ F_{ms,0} &= Q_{ms,\rm ISO} B^3 R^3 \tilde{N_0} , \\ F_{c,0} &= Q_{c,\rm ISO} B^3 R^3 \tilde{N_0} , \\ F_{m,0} &= Q_{m,\rm ISO} B^3 R^3 \tilde{N_0} , \end{aligned}\end{split}\]

where

\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} c_{1,\rm ISO} &= \frac{3 q_e}{2\pi^2 m_e c}, \\ Q_{m0,\rm ISO} &= \frac{4}{3}\pi f_V \chi_{\rm ISO} \gamma_m^{1-p} D_L^{-2}, \\ Q_{ms,\rm ISO} &= \frac{4}{3}\pi f_V \chi_{\rm ISO} \gamma_m^{1-p} \gamma_c^p D_L^{-2}, \\ Q_{c,\rm ISO} &= \frac{4}{3}\pi f_V \chi_{\rm ISO} \gamma_m^2 \gamma_c^{-1} D_L^{-2}. \end{aligned}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} \nu_{\rm synchrotron}(\gamma) &= c_{1} B \gamma^2 \sin\alpha, \\ F_{ms,0} &= Q_{ms,0} B^3 R^3 \tilde{N_0} \sin\alpha, \\ F_{c,0} &= Q_{c,0} B^3 R^3 \tilde{N_0} \sin\alpha, \\ F_{m,0} &= Q_{m,0} B^3 R^3 \tilde{N_0} \sin\alpha, \end{aligned}\end{split}\]

where

\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} c_{1} &= \frac{3 q_e}{4\pi m_e c}, \\ Q_{m,0} &= \frac{4}{3}\pi f_V \chi \gamma_m^{1-p} D_L^{-2}, \\ Q_{c,0} &= \frac{4}{3}\pi f_V \chi \gamma_m^2 \gamma_c^{-1} D_L^{-2} \\ Q_{ms,0} &= \frac{4}{3}\pi f_V \chi \gamma_m^{1-p} \gamma_c^p D_L^{-2}. \end{aligned}\end{split}\]

Under these assumptions, each of the SED forms described above may be explicitly inverted.

Note

Going forward, we present the fixed pitch angle case, but present critical results in both scenarios.

Inverting from Optically Thin Peaks#

The easiest case to describe is the inversion based on the SEDs with optically thin peaks at either \(\nu_m\) or \(\nu_c\). In these cases, the break frequency is directly related to the characteristic synchrotron frequency of electrons at either \(\gamma_m\) or \(\gamma_c\), and the flux normalization at the break frequency is directly related to the optically thin normalization at that frequency. This allows for a straightforward inversion to physical parameters.

To begin the process, we recognize that the break frequency is related to the Lorentz factor of the electrons responsible for the emission at that frequency through the characteristic synchrotron frequency:

(13)#\[\nu_m = \frac{3 q B \sin \alpha}{4 \pi m c}\left[\gamma_{\min}\right]^2 = c_{1} B \sin \alpha \gamma_{\min}^2.\]

This immediately allows us to invert for the magnetic field strength as

(14)#\[\boxed{ B = \frac{\nu_m}{c_{1,\rm ISO} \gamma_{\min}^2}, }\]
(15)#\[\boxed{ B \sin \alpha = \frac{\nu_m}{c_{1} \gamma_{\min}^2}. }\]

The flux normalization at the break frequency may then be used to determine the radius:

(16)#\[\boxed{ R = \left(\frac{F_{m,0}}{Q_{m,\rm ISO} B^3 \tilde{N_0}}\right)^{1/3}, }\]
(17)#\[\boxed{ R = \left(\frac{F_{0}}{Q_{0} B^3 \tilde{N_0} \sin \alpha}\right)^{1/3}, }\]

where \(F_0\) and \(Q_0\) are the appropriate normalization flux and constant for the cooling regime in question (i.e., \(F_{m,0}\) and \(Q_{m,0}\) for slow cooling and \(F_{c,0}\) and \(Q_{c,0}\) for fast cooling).

A similar technique can be employed when the break appears at the cooling frequency instead of the injection frequency with the exception that one must replace \(\gamma_m\) with \(\gamma_c\) when computing the magnetic field.

Inverting for Optically Thick Peaks#

When the SSA frequency \(\nu_a\) sets the observed peak, the inversion process is more complex. In this case, the observed peak frequency and flux are not directly related to the characteristic synchrotron frequency of a particular electron population, and the flux normalization at the peak frequency is not directly related to the optically thin normalization. Instead, one must use the known shape of the SED and the relevant break frequencies to “extrapolate” from the optically thin normalization to the observed peak frequency and flux.

Regardless of the spectral regime, the peak flux will correspond to the blackbody flux at \(\nu_a\):

(18)#\[F_\nu(\nu_a) = 2\nu_a^2 m_e \gamma_a \Omega = 2 m_e \Omega \gamma_m \left(\frac{\nu_a}{\nu_m}\right)^{1/2} \nu_a^2.\]

Defining \(P_0\) such that

\[P_{0,\rm ISO} = 2\pi m_e f_A D_A^{-2} c_{1,\rm ISO}^{-1/2},\]
\[P_0 = 2\pi m_e f_A D_A^{-2} c_{1}^{-1/2} \sin^{-1/2} \alpha,\]

the result is a general condition for the SSA frequency:

\[F_{\rm brk} \nu_{\rm brk}^{-5/2} = P_0 R^2 B^{-1/2}\]

A second condition may be obtained by requiring that the peak flux at the peak frequency be consistent with the normalization as described in the previous section and used in the optically thin case. I general, this results in a set of equations

\[F_{\rm brk} \nu_{\rm brk}^{-5/2} = P_0 R^2 B^{-1/2}, F_{\rm brk}^\alpha \nu_{\rm brk}^\beta = C R^\gamma B^\delta,\]

where \(C\) is a constant derived from the normalization and \(\alpha\), \(\beta\), \(\gamma\), and \(\delta\) are constants which depend on the cooling regime and the spectral regime. Solving this system of equations then allows for the inversion to physical parameters. The case is solved explicitly for each of the relevant regimes in their corresponding documentation pages.

Another simple scenario worth considering is the SED from a synchrotron source with non-negligible cooling and no SSA. In this case, the three relevant break frequencies are \(\nu_m\), \(\nu_c\), and \(\nu_{\rm max}\). There are 3 possible configurations

  1. \(\nu_c < \nu_m < \nu_{\rm max}\): The fast-cooling regime. The SED here is composed of segments SPL E, SPL F, SPL H, and SPL I with slopes \(1/3, 1/2, -p/2, {\rm exp}\). The maximum in this case occurs at \(\nu_c\), and so we use that point to normalize.

  2. \(\nu_m < \nu_c < \nu_{\rm max}\): The slow-cooling regime. The SED here is composed of segments SPL D, SPL G, SPL H, and SPL I with slopes \(1/3, -(p-1)/2, -p/2, {\rm exp}\). The maximum in this case occurs at \(\nu_m\), and so we use that point to normalize.

  3. \(\nu_m < \nu_{\rm max} < \nu_c\): The uncooled regime. The SED here is identical to the standard power-law SED and is therefore not described in any further detail.

Cooling + Absorption SEDs#

We are now prepared to introduce the complete set of synchrotron SEDs relevant to the most generic scenarios in which both SSA and cooling are relevant. We therefore have the break frequencies \(\nu_m\), \(\nu_a\), \(\nu_c\), and \(\nu_{\rm max}\). In addition, for absorption dominated regimes with fast cooling, we have the additional break frequency \(\nu_{\rm ac}\) from stratified SSA (see the theory note: Theory Note: Stratified Synchrotron Absorption). This leads to 8 regimes characterized by the cooling state and the radiation transfer state at maximum:

  • A spectrum is either fast cooling (\(\nu_c < \nu_m\)), slow cooling (\(\nu_m <\nu_c < \nu_{\rm max}\)) or no cooling (\(\nu_c > \nu_{\rm max}\)).

  • A spectrum is optically thick at maximum if \(\nu_a > \rm{min}(\nu_a,\nu_c)\) and is optically thin at peak if \(\nu_a < \rm{min}(\nu_a,\nu_c)\).

The resulting spectra are

  1. \((\nu_a < \nu_m < \nu_{\rm max} < \nu_c)\): This is the thin, no cooling spectrum.

  2. \((\nu_m < \nu_a < \nu_{\rm max} < \nu_c)\): This it the thick, no cooling spectrum.

  3. \((\nu_a < \nu_m < \nu_c < \nu_{\rm max})\): This is the thin, slow cooling spectrum.

  4. \((\nu_m < \nu_a < \nu_c < \nu_{\rm max})\): This is the thick, slow cooling spectrum.

  5. \((\nu_a < \nu_c < \nu_m < \nu_{\rm max})\): This is the thin, fast cooling spectrum.

  6. \((\nu_c < \nu_a < \nu_m < \nu_{\rm max})\): This is the thick, fast cooling spectrum.

  7. \((\nu_c, \nu_m < \nu_a < \nu_{\rm max})\): This is the extremely thick, fast cooling spectrum.

The Absorption Frequency

In this case, the absorption frequency \(\nu_a\) does not correspond to the peak of the SED. Instead, the spectrum peaks at the cooling break \(\nu_c\), with the absorption break occurring at lower frequency, \(\nu_a < \nu_c < \nu_m\). As a result, the flux density at \(\nu_a\) must be obtained by propagating downward from the peak using the appropriate optically thin power-law segment.

Between \(\nu_a\) and \(\nu_c\), the spectrum follows a \(\nu^{1/3}\) scaling. The flux density on the optically thin side at the absorption frequency is therefore

\[F_\nu(\nu_a) = F_{\nu,\rm pk} \left(\frac{\nu_a}{\nu_c}\right)^{1/3},\]

where \(F_{\nu,\rm pk}\) denotes the peak flux density at \(\nu_c\).

On the optically thick side, the emission at \(\nu_a\) is well approximated by a blackbody with effective temperature \(kT_{\rm eff} = \gamma_a m_e c^2\), where the Lorentz factor of the emitting electrons satisfies

\[\gamma_a = \gamma_m \left(\frac{\nu_a}{\nu_m}\right)^{1/2}.\]

The corresponding optically thick flux density is therefore

\[F_\nu(\nu_a) = 2\nu_a^2 m_e \gamma_a \Omega = 2 m_e \Omega \gamma_m \left(\frac{\nu_a}{\nu_m}\right)^{1/2} \nu_a^2.\]

Equating the optically thin and optically thick expressions at \(\nu_a\) yields

\[F_{\nu,\rm pk} \left(\frac{\nu_a}{\nu_c}\right)^{1/3} = 2 m_e \Omega \gamma_m \nu_m^{-1/2} \nu_a^{5/2}.\]

Solving for the absorption frequency, we obtain

\[\boxed{ \nu_a = \left(\frac{F_{\nu,\rm pk}}{2 m_e \Omega \gamma_m}\right)^{6/13} \nu_m^{3/13} \nu_c^{-2/13}. }\]

This expression makes explicit that, in this spectral ordering, the absorption frequency depends not only on the peak flux density and angular size of the source, but also on the location of the cooling break, reflecting the fact that the SED peak occurs at \(\nu_c\) rather than at the absorption frequency itself.

\[F_\nu = F_{\nu_{\rm pk},0} \, F_\nu^{(B,A)}(\nu;\nu_m) \, \tilde{F}_\nu^{(A,H)}(\nu;\nu_a) \, \tilde{\Phi}(\nu;\nu_{\max})\]

The Absorption Frequency

In this case, the absorption frequency \(\nu_a\) corresponds to the peak-frequency of the SED. The flux from the optically thin portion of the SED at \(\nu_a\) is given by \(F_{\nu,\rm pk}\). The optically thick side must be

\[F_{nu,\rm pk} = 2\nu_a^2 \gamma_a m_e \Omega = 2\nu_a^2 m_e \Omega \left(\frac{\nu_a}{\nu_m}\right)^{1/2} \gamma_m,\]

where we make use of \(\gamma_m\) as a hyper-parameter to relate \(\gamma_a\) and \(\nu_a\). Thus,

\[\boxed{ \nu_a = \left(\frac{F_{\nu,\rm pk}}{2 m_e \Omega \gamma_m}\right)^{2/5} \nu_m^{1/5}. }\]

In this case, the absorption frequency lies above both the cooling and the injection frequencies. Therefore, the true peak of the SED occurs at \(\nu_a\) and the normalization must be propagated. It turns out that, by coincidence, the propagation is identical whether we are fast cooling or slow cooling; however, the normalization of the underlying electron population is different in each case (as is the resulting normalization flux).

Let \(F_{0}\) be the appropriate normalization at \(\nu_m\) (slow cooling). The true peak of the SED occurs at \(\nu_a\) and the normalization must be propagated from \(\nu_m\) up to \(\nu_a\).

\[F_{\rm pk} = F_{c,0} \left(\frac{\nu_c}{\nu_m}\right)^{-(p-1)/2} \left(\frac{\nu_a}{\nu_c}\right)^{-p/2} = F_{c,0} \nu_m^{(p-1)/2} \nu_c^{1/2} \nu_a^{-p/2}.\]

The absorption frequency in this regime is given by the SSA matching condition derived above,

\[\nu_a = \left(\frac{F_{\nu,\rm pk}}{2 m_e \Omega \gamma_m}\right)^{2/5} \nu_m^{1/5}.\]

With these expressions, we may solve self-consistently for \(\nu_a\):

\[\nu_a = \left(\frac{F_{c,0}}{2 m_e \Omega \gamma_m}\right)^{\frac{2}{p+5}} \nu_m^{\frac{p}{p+5}} \nu_c^{\frac{1}{p+5}}.\]

Because the SSA frequency corresponds to the peak, we have the standard equation

The inversion for the magnetic field strength and radius in the isotropic pitch-angle case is given by

\[F_{\rm brk} \nu_{\rm brk}^{-5/2} = P_{0,\rm ISO} R^2 B^{-1/2}\]

The inversion for the magnetic field strength and radius in the fixed pitch-angle case is given by

\[F_{\rm brk} \nu_{\rm brk}^{-5/2} = P_{0} R^2 B^{-1/2},\]

where \(P_0\) absorbs the pitch-angle dependence.

The second closure in this case appears because the peak frequency determined from the normalization must match that observed in the data. From above, we have the relationship that

\[F_{\rm pk} = F_{\rm c, 0} \nu_m^{(p-1)/2} \nu_c^{1/2} \nu_a^{-p/2},\]

As such, we have the condition that

\[\nu_{\rm brk}^{p/2} F_{\rm pk} = c_1^{p/2} \gamma_m^{p-1} \gamma_c \sin^{p+2/2} \alpha Q_{c,\rm 0} \tilde{N}_0 R^3 B^{(p+6)/2}.\]

Letting

\[A = c_1^{p/2} \gamma_m^{p-1} \gamma_c \sin^{p+2/2} \alpha Q_{c,0} \tilde{N}_0,\]

we have the coupled equations

\[\boxed{ F_{\rm brk} \nu_{\rm brk}^{-5/2} = P_{0} R^2 B^{-1/2}, \qquad \nu_{\rm brk}^{p/2} F_{\rm pk} = A R^3 B^{(p+6)/2} }\]

The solution to which is

\[\begin{split}\boxed{ \begin{aligned} R &= A^{-1/(2p+15)} P_0^{-(p+6)/(2p+15)} F_{\rm brk}^{(p+7)/(2p+15)} \nu_{\rm brk}^{-1}\\ B &= A^{-4/(2p+15)} P_0^{6/(2p+15)} F_{\rm brk}^{-2/(2p+15)} \nu_{\rm brk}, \end{aligned} }\end{split}\]

where

\[A = c_1^{p/2} \gamma_m^{p-1} \gamma_c \sin^{p+2/2} \alpha Q_{c,0} \tilde{N}_0,\]
\[\begin{split}\boxed{ \begin{aligned} R &= A^{-1/(2p+15)} P_{0,\rm ISO}^{-(p+6)/(2p+15)} F_{\rm brk}^{(p+7)/(2p+15)} \nu_{\rm brk}^{-1}\\ B &= A^{-4/(2p+15)} P_{0,\rm ISO}^{6/(2p+15)} F_{\rm brk}^{-2/(2p+15)} \nu_{\rm brk}, \end{aligned} }\end{split}\]
