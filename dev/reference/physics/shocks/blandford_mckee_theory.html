Theory: The Blandford–McKee Solution#

The Blandford–McKee solution describes the self-similar evolution of an ultra-relativistic blastwave expanding into an external medium[1]. It is the relativistic analog of the Sedov–Taylor blastwave[2], but with an important physical difference: the shocked material is compressed into a very thin shell behind the shock front.

This solution is especially useful for modeling relativistic explosions such as gamma-ray burst afterglows and other transients in which the shocked material moves with Lorentz factor \(\Gamma \gg 1\).

Overview and Assumptions#

We consider a relativistic blastwave produced by an explosion with (lab-frame) energy \(E\), expanding into an initially cold external medium. The blastwave is relativistic when the explosion energy greatly exceeds the rest-mass energy of the swept-up material,

\[E \gg \left(M_{\rm ej} + \rho V\right)c^2.\]

Equivalently, most of the energy is carried as bulk kinetic energy and internal energy of relativistically moving shocked gas.

Model Assumptions

  • The upstream external medium is cold and initially at rest in the lab frame.

  • The blastwave is adiabatic: there is no radiative energy loss and no external work other than sweeping up ambient material.

  • The shocked gas is ultra-relativistically hot, so its equation of state is

    \[e = 3p, \qquad w=e+p=4p,\]

    where \(e\) is the proper internal energy density, \(p\) is the pressure, and \(w\) is the relativistic enthalpy density.

  • The shock Lorentz factor satisfies \(\Gamma \gg 1\).

  • The shocked region is geometrically thin,

    \[\Delta R \sim \frac{R}{\Gamma^2} \ll R.\]

The solution is self-similar because, in the ultra-relativistic limit, the structure behind the shock depends only on the distance behind the shock in units of the relativistic shell thickness.

Relativistic Shock Jump Conditions#

Let \(\Gamma\) be the Lorentz factor of the shock measured in the rest frame of the unshocked upstream gas. In the strong, ultra-relativistic limit, the relativistic Rankine–Hugoniot conditions (see Methods: (Relativistic) Jump Conditions) imply that the downstream gas immediately behind the shock has

\[\gamma_2^2 \simeq \frac{\Gamma^2}{2},\]
\[p_2 \simeq \frac{2}{3}w_1\Gamma^2,\]

and

\[n_2' \simeq 2\Gamma^2 n_1.\]

Here \(\gamma_2\) is the Lorentz factor of the shocked gas in the lab frame, \(p_2\) is the downstream pressure, \(w_1\) is the upstream enthalpy density, \(n_1\) is the upstream proper number density, and \(n_2'\) is the downstream lab-frame number density. For a cold upstream medium,

\[w_1 \simeq \rho_1 c^2.\]

These relations provide the natural normalizations for the Blandford–McKee self-similar profiles. Their derivation, including the full set of relativistic flux-conservation equations and the analytic \(\beta_2 = 1/3\) result for a cold upstream medium, is worked through in Methods: (Relativistic) Jump Conditions.

Relativistic Hydrodynamic Equations#

The dynamics of the shocked flow follow from stress-energy conservation,

\[\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=0.\]

For a perfect fluid,

\[T^{\mu\nu} = w u^\mu u^\nu + p g^{\mu\nu},\]

where

\[w=e+p, \qquad u^\mu=\gamma(1,\beta,0,0), \qquad \gamma=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta^2}}.\]

In spherical symmetry, and using units with \(c=1\), energy conservation is

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left[ (e+p)\gamma^2-p \right] + \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left[ r^2(e+p)\gamma^2\beta \right] = 0,\]

while radial momentum conservation is

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left[ (e+p)\gamma^2\beta \right] + \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left[ r^2(e+p)\gamma^2\beta^2 \right] + \frac{\partial p}{\partial r} = 0.\]

For the ultra-relativistic equation of state,

\[e=3p, \qquad w=4p,\]

these equations may be recast into the useful Lagrangian form

\[\frac{d}{dt}\left(p\gamma^4\right) = \gamma^2\frac{\partial p}{\partial t},\]

and

\[\frac{d}{dt} \log\left(p^3\gamma^4\right) = -\frac{4}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left(r^2\beta\right),\]

where

\[\frac{d}{dt} = \frac{\partial}{\partial t} + \beta\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\]

is the derivative following the flow.

Baryon number conservation gives

\[\nabla_\mu(nu^\mu)=0.\]

In spherical symmetry,

\[\frac{\partial n'}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left(r^2 n'\beta\right) = 0,\]

where

\[n'=\gamma n\]

is the lab-frame number density and \(n\) is the proper comoving density. Combining baryon conservation with the ultra-relativistic equation of state gives

\[\frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{p}{n^{4/3}} \right) = 0.\]

This is simply conservation of entropy along each fluid worldline.

Thickness of the Relativistic Shell#

The first major difference between the Blandford–McKee solution and the Sedov–Taylor solution is the thickness of the shocked region.

In a non-relativistic blastwave, the shocked region occupies an order-unity fraction of the shock radius,

\[\Delta R \sim R.\]

In an ultra-relativistic blastwave, the shocked material is compressed into a thin shell. A simple mass-conservation argument gives this thickness.

The swept-up rest mass inside radius \(R\) is

\[M_{\rm sw} \sim \frac{4\pi}{3}R^3\rho_1.\]

If this material is concentrated into a shell of lab-frame thickness \(\Delta R\), then

\[M_{\rm sw} \sim 4\pi R^2\Delta R\,\rho_2',\]

where \(\rho_2'\) is the downstream lab-frame density. Since the shock jump conditions imply

\[\rho_2' \sim \Gamma^2\rho_1,\]

we obtain

\[\frac{4\pi}{3}R^3\rho_1 \sim 4\pi R^2\Delta R\,\Gamma^2\rho_1.\]

Thus,

\[\Delta R \sim \frac{R}{\Gamma^2}.\]

Key Result: Relativistic Shell Thickness

\[\Delta R \sim \frac{R}{\Gamma^2}\ll R.\]

This thin-shell structure motivates a similarity coordinate that resolves distances behind the shock in units of \(R/\Gamma^2\), rather than in units of \(R\).

Deceleration Law#

For an adiabatic relativistic blastwave, the total energy is conserved. The swept-up mass scales as

\[M_{\rm sw}\sim \rho_1 R^3.\]

For an ultra-relativistic shock, the energy per unit swept-up rest mass is enhanced by a factor of order \(\Gamma^2\), so

\[E \sim \Gamma^2 M_{\rm sw}c^2 \sim \Gamma^2\rho_1R^3c^2.\]

For a uniform external medium and fixed explosion energy,

\[\Gamma^2R^3 = \text{constant}.\]

Since the shock is ultra-relativistic,

\[R\simeq ct,\]

and therefore

\[\Gamma^2 \propto t^{-3}.\]

More generally, we write

\[\Gamma^2 \propto t^{-m},\]

where \(m\) is a deceleration index. The adiabatic, energy-conserving blastwave in a uniform external medium has

\[m=3.\]

The shock radius may be written, to the order needed to resolve the shell, as

\[R(t) = ct \left[ 1-\frac{1}{2(m+1)\Gamma^2} \right].\]

In units with \(c=1\),

\[R(t) = t \left[ 1-\frac{1}{2(m+1)\Gamma^2} \right].\]

The correction is small, of order \(\Gamma^{-2}\), but it is the same order as the fractional shell thickness and therefore must be retained in the self-similar coordinate.

The Blandford–McKee Similarity Variable#

A Sedov–Taylor-like coordinate,

\[\xi_{\rm ST}=\frac{r}{R(t)},\]

is not useful in the relativistic problem because nearly all of the shocked fluid lies in an extremely narrow region near \(\xi_{\rm ST}=1\).

Instead, define a coordinate that measures distance behind the shock in units of the relativistic shell thickness:

\[\xi = \left(1-\frac{r}{R}\right)\Gamma^2.\]

Since

\[1-\frac{r}{R} = \frac{R-r}{R},\]

we have

\[\xi = \frac{R-r}{R/\Gamma^2}.\]

Thus, \(\xi\) is order unity throughout the shocked shell.

Blandford and McKee define the closely related coordinate

\[\chi = 1+2(m+1)\Gamma^2 \left(1-\frac{r}{R}\right).\]

The shock front is located at

\[r=R \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \chi=1.\]

Material farther behind the shock has larger \(\chi\).

Key Result: Blandford–McKee Similarity Coordinate

\[\chi = 1+2(m+1)\Gamma^2 \left(1-\frac{r}{R}\right).\]

The numerical factor \(2(m+1)\) is chosen for algebraic convenience. The essential physical point is that \(\chi\) resolves the thin relativistic shell rather than the full blastwave radius.

Self-Similar Ansatz#

The shock jump conditions provide natural normalizations for the downstream profiles. We introduce dimensionless functions \(f(\chi)\), \(g(\chi)\), and \(h(\chi)\) by

\[p(r,t) = \frac{2}{3}w_1\Gamma^2(t) f(\chi),\]
\[\gamma^2(r,t) = \frac{1}{2}\Gamma^2(t) g(\chi),\]

and

\[n'(r,t) = 2n_1\Gamma^2(t) h(\chi).\]

The shock boundary conditions are then

\[f(1)=g(1)=h(1)=1.\]

Substituting this ansatz into the relativistic hydrodynamic equations reduces the partial differential equations to ordinary differential equations in \(\chi\).

For the adiabatic energy-conserving solution in a uniform external medium, \(m=3\), the Blandford–McKee profiles are

\[g(\chi)=\chi^{-1},\]
\[f(\chi)=\chi^{-17/12},\]

and

\[h(\chi)=\chi^{-7/4}.\]

Thus,

\[\gamma^2(r,t) = \frac{1}{2}\Gamma^2\chi^{-1},\]
\[p(r,t) = \frac{2}{3}w_1\Gamma^2\chi^{-17/12},\]

and

\[n'(r,t) = 2n_1\Gamma^2\chi^{-7/4}.\]

Range of the Similarity Variable#

The shocked region has thickness

\[\Delta R \sim \frac{R}{\Gamma^2}.\]

Therefore, throughout the main shocked shell,

\[1-\frac{r}{R} \sim \frac{1}{\Gamma^2}.\]

The similarity coordinate then satisfies

\[\chi-1 = 2(m+1)\Gamma^2 \left(1-\frac{r}{R}\right) \sim 2(m+1).\]

For the adiabatic uniform-medium solution, \(m=3\), so

\[\chi \sim 1+8\]

across the main shell, up to factors of order unity. The formal similarity solution may be evaluated to larger \(\chi\), but most of the energy is concentrated in the relativistically thin region behind the shock.

Energy Normalization#

The total blastwave energy is obtained by integrating the lab-frame energy density over the shocked region. For an ultra-relativistic gas,

\[T^{00} = (e+p)\gamma^2-p = 4p\gamma^2-p \simeq 4p\gamma^2,\]

because \(\gamma^2\gg1\).

The energy is therefore

\[E = 4\pi \int_0^R 4p\gamma^2r^2\,dr.\]

Using the similarity forms,

\[p = \frac{2}{3}w_1\Gamma^2 f(\chi),\]

and

\[\gamma^2 = \frac{1}{2}\Gamma^2 g(\chi),\]

we find

\[4p\gamma^2 = \frac{4}{3}w_1\Gamma^4 f(\chi)g(\chi).\]

The coordinate transformation is, to leading order,

\[d\chi = -2(m+1)\Gamma^2\frac{dr}{R},\]

so

\[dr = -\frac{R}{2(m+1)\Gamma^2}d\chi.\]

Because the shell is thin, \(r\simeq R\) inside the integral. Therefore,

\[E \simeq \frac{8\pi}{3(m+1)} w_1\Gamma^2R^3 \int_1^{\chi_{\rm max}} f(\chi)g(\chi)\,d\chi.\]

For the adiabatic uniform-medium solution,

\[f(\chi)g(\chi) = \chi^{-17/12}\chi^{-1} = \chi^{-29/12}.\]

The integral converges:

\[\int_1^\infty \chi^{-29/12}d\chi = \frac{12}{17}.\]

Since \(m=3\), we obtain

\[E = \frac{8\pi}{17} w_1\Gamma^2R^3.\]

For a cold upstream medium,

\[w_1\simeq\rho_1c^2,\]

so

\[E = \frac{8\pi}{17} \rho_1c^2\Gamma^2R^3.\]

Solving for the shock Lorentz factor gives

\[\Gamma^2(R) = \frac{17E}{8\pi\rho_1c^2R^3}.\]

Since \(R\simeq ct\), this reproduces the scaling

\[\Gamma^2\propto t^{-3}.\]

Key Result: Blandford–McKee Energy Normalization

\[E = \frac{8\pi}{17} \rho_1c^2\Gamma^2R^3.\]

Equivalently,

\[\Gamma^2(R) = \frac{17E}{8\pi\rho_1c^2R^3}.\]
