Theory: Numerical Shock Engines#

In many transient applications, the shock evolution is not well approximated by a self-similar solution. Self-similar models are extremely useful when the ejecta and circumstellar medium (CSM) have simple power-law structures, but real systems may have arbitrary ejecta profiles, structured winds, finite shells, density jumps, or other environmental features.

The numerical shock engines evolve reduced, low-dimensional models for the shock dynamics by integrating a small set of ordinary differential equations. They do not resolve the full hydrodynamic structure of the shocked gas; instead, they replace the shocked interaction region by a compact set of global variables whose evolution is governed by conservation laws and closure assumptions.

This page develops the theory behind the numerical shock models implemented in trilobite.dynamics.shocks.numerical, organized into non-relativistic and relativistic families.

Non-relativistic models (\(v \ll c\)):

  1. the conservative snowplow model (MomentumConservingShockEngine);

  2. the pressure-driven thin-shell model (PressureDrivenThinShellShockEngine);

  3. the mechanical internal-energy model (MechanicalShockEngine).

Relativistic models (arbitrary \(\beta\), Lorentz-invariant jump conditions):

  1. the relativistic pressure-driven thin-shell model (RelPressureDrivenThinShellShockEngine).

Each model makes different assumptions about the relevant physics and is applicable in different regimes.

Overview#

While each of the closures described in this document differ in their physics, assumptions, and implementations, they share a common derivation starting point: conservation laws integrated over a moving spherical control volume. Below we establish the geometry and derive the mass, momentum, and energy equations that serve as the common foundation for all three models.

Geometry#

In establishing these models, we treat the typical geometry of a shock driven by an expanding spherical outflow into an ambient medium. We use the standard four-region notation for a supernova–CSM interaction:

\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} 1 &: \text{unshocked ejecta},\\ 2 &: \text{shocked ejecta},\\ 3 &: \text{shocked CSM},\\ 4 &: \text{unshocked CSM}. \end{aligned}\end{split}\]

The reverse shock, contact discontinuity, and forward shock are located at

\[R_{\rm rs}(t) < R_{\rm cd}(t) < R_{\rm fs}(t),\]

with speeds

\[D_{\rm rs} = \dot R_{\rm rs}, \qquad v_{\rm cd} = \dot R_{\rm cd}, \qquad D_{\rm fs} = \dot R_{\rm fs}.\]

The upstream ejecta and CSM are prescribed by

\[\rho_1(r,t),\qquad u_1(r,t), \qquad \rho_4(r,t),\qquad u_4(r,t).\]

Unless stated otherwise, the upstream media are assumed to be cold,

\[P_1 \simeq 0, \qquad P_4 \simeq 0.\]

Hint

For homologously expanding ejecta,

\[u_1(r,t) = \frac{r}{t},\]

and the ejecta density can be written as

\[\rho_1(r,t) = t^{-3}G\!\left(\frac{r}{t}\right).\]

Equivalently, if the ejecta mass distribution in velocity space is

\[\frac{dM_{\rm ej}}{dv}=f(v),\]

then

\[G(v) = \frac{f(v)}{4\pi v^2}.\]

Equations of Motion#

The Conservation Equation#

The continuity equation in spherical symmetry is

\[\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left(r^2\rho u\right) = 0.\]

Define the mass in the moving control volume \(a(t)<r<b(t)\) as

\[M_{[a,b]} = 4\pi \int_{a(t)}^{b(t)} \rho r^2\,dr.\]

Multiplying the continuity equation by \(4\pi r^2\) and integrating over \([a,b]\) using the Leibniz rule gives the moving-control-volume mass equation,

\[\boxed{ \frac{dM_{[a,b]}}{dt} = 4\pi a^2 \left[ \rho(u-\dot a) \right]_{r=a} - 4\pi b^2 \left[ \rho(u-\dot b) \right]_{r=b}. }\]

Each term represents the net mass flux through a moving boundary: mass entering from the inner face at \(r=a\) with relative inflow velocity \(u-\dot a\), and leaving through the outer face at \(r=b\) with relative outflow velocity \(u-\dot b\).

Derivation: moving-boundary mass equation

Multiplying the continuity equation by \(4\pi r^2\),

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(4\pi r^2\rho\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(4\pi r^2\rho u\right) = 0.\]

Integrating over \(a(t)<r<b(t)\),

\[\int_a^b \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left(4\pi r^2\rho\right)\,dr + \left[ 4\pi r^2\rho u \right]_a^b = 0.\]

By the Leibniz rule,

\[\frac{d}{dt} \int_a^b 4\pi r^2\rho\,dr = \int_a^b \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left(4\pi r^2\rho\right)\,dr + 4\pi b^2\rho|_b\,\dot b - 4\pi a^2\rho|_a\,\dot a.\]

Substituting and rearranging gives

\[\frac{dM_{[a,b]}}{dt} = 4\pi a^2 \left[ \rho(u-\dot a) \right]_{r=a} - 4\pi b^2 \left[ \rho(u-\dot b) \right]_{r=b}.\]

The Momentum Equation#

The non-relativistic radial momentum equation in spherical symmetry is

\[\frac{\partial(\rho u)}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left[ r^2(\rho u^2+P) \right] = \frac{2P}{r}.\]

Define the momentum in the control volume \(a(t)<r<b(t)\) as

\[\Pi_{[a,b]} = 4\pi \int_{a(t)}^{b(t)} \rho u r^2\,dr.\]

The moving-control-volume momentum equation is

\[\begin{split}\boxed{ \begin{aligned} \frac{d\Pi_{[a,b]}}{dt} &= 4\pi a^2 \left[ \rho u(u-\dot a)+P \right]_{r=a} - 4\pi b^2 \left[ \rho u(u-\dot b)+P \right]_{r=b} \\[4pt] &\qquad + 8\pi \int_a^b P r\,dr. \end{aligned} }\end{split}\]

The first two terms are boundary momentum fluxes plus boundary pressure forces; the last term is the spherical-geometry pressure contribution that vanishes in the plane-parallel limit.

Derivation: moving-boundary momentum equation

Multiplying the momentum equation by \(4\pi r^2\),

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left(4\pi r^2\rho u\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left[ 4\pi r^2(\rho u^2+P) \right] = 8\pi rP.\]

Integrating over \(a(t)<r<b(t)\),

\[\int_a^b \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left(4\pi r^2\rho u\right)\,dr + \left[ 4\pi r^2(\rho u^2+P) \right]_a^b = 8\pi\int_a^b Pr\,dr.\]

By the Leibniz rule,

\[\frac{d}{dt} \int_a^b 4\pi r^2\rho u\,dr = \int_a^b \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left(4\pi r^2\rho u\right)\,dr + 4\pi b^2\rho u|_b\,\dot b - 4\pi a^2\rho u|_a\,\dot a.\]

Rearranging gives

\[\frac{d\Pi_{[a,b]}}{dt} = 4\pi a^2 \left[ \rho u(u-\dot a)+P \right]_a - 4\pi b^2 \left[ \rho u(u-\dot b)+P \right]_b + 8\pi \int_a^b Pr\,dr.\]

The Energy Equation#

Define the total energy density as

\[E = \frac{1}{2}\rho u^2 + \rho\epsilon,\]

where \(\epsilon\) is the specific internal energy. In inviscid spherical flow with a volumetric source term \(\dot{q}\) (e.g., radiative cooling),

\[\frac{\partial E}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left[r^2(E+P)u\right] = \dot{q}.\]

Define the total energy in the control volume \(a(t)<r<b(t)\) as

\[\mathcal{E}_{[a,b]} = 4\pi\int_{a(t)}^{b(t)} E r^2\,dr.\]

The moving-control-volume energy equation is

\[\boxed{ \frac{d\mathcal{E}_{[a,b]}}{dt} = 4\pi a^2 \left[ (E+P)(u-\dot a) \right]_{r=a} - 4\pi b^2 \left[ (E+P)(u-\dot b) \right]_{r=b} + \mathcal{Q}_{[a,b]}, }\]

where \(\mathcal{Q}_{[a,b]} = 4\pi\int_a^b \dot{q}\,r^2\,dr\) is the net volumetric source integrated over the control volume. The energy flux through each boundary involves the enthalpy \(E + P\) rather than the internal energy alone, reflecting the \(P\,dV\) work done as mass crosses each face.

Note

The three numerical models treat the energy equation differently:

  • The conservative snowplow model discards the energy equation entirely; shock-generated thermal energy is assumed to be radiated away immediately and no internal energy is retained.

  • The pressure-driven thin-shell model replaces the energy equation with algebraic Rankine–Hugoniot expressions for the post-shock pressures; no internal energy is evolved.

  • The mechanical model evolves separate internal energies \(U_2\) and \(U_3\) for the shocked ejecta and shocked CSM as independent ODEs.

Derivation: moving-boundary energy equation

Multiplying the energy equation by \(4\pi r^2\),

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(4\pi r^2 E\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left[4\pi r^2(E+P)u\right] = 4\pi r^2\dot{q}.\]

Integrating over \(a(t)<r<b(t)\),

\[\int_a^b \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(4\pi r^2 E\right)\,dr + \left[4\pi r^2(E+P)u\right]_a^b = \mathcal{Q}_{[a,b]}.\]

By the Leibniz rule,

\[\frac{d}{dt} \int_a^b 4\pi r^2 E\,dr = \int_a^b \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(4\pi r^2 E\right)\,dr + 4\pi b^2 E|_b\,\dot b - 4\pi a^2 E|_a\,\dot a.\]

Rearranging gives

\[\frac{d\mathcal{E}_{[a,b]}}{dt} = 4\pi a^2 \left[ (E+P)(u-\dot a) \right]_{r=a} - 4\pi b^2 \left[ (E+P)(u-\dot b) \right]_{r=b} + \mathcal{Q}_{[a,b]}.\]

Momentum-Conserving Snowplow#

The momentum-conserving snowplow (MomentumConservingShockEngine) is the simplest of the three numerical closures. It collapses the entire shocked interaction region into a single razor-thin shell of mass \(M_{\rm sh}\) and evolves that shell purely through conservation of mass and momentum. No internal energy is retained: shock-generated thermal energy is assumed to be radiated away immediately, so the shell remains cold and its only dynamical degree of freedom is its bulk momentum.

This closure is the natural description of a radiative snowplow — the phase of shock evolution in which cooling is so efficient that the post-shock gas cannot remain hot and instead accumulates in a thin, dense, cold slab.

Assumptions#

  • The shocked region (\(R_{\rm rs} < r < R_{\rm fs}\)) collapses to a razor-thin shell at a single radius \(R_{\rm sh}\), so that \(R_{\rm rs}\simeq R_{\rm cd}\simeq R_{\rm fs}\equiv R_{\rm sh}\).

  • The shell velocity equals the contact-discontinuity velocity: \(D_{\rm rs}\simeq v_{\rm cd}\simeq D_{\rm fs}\equiv v_{\rm sh}\).

  • Shock-generated thermal energy is radiated instantly; no pressure reservoir is retained.

  • Mass and momentum are conserved; the energy equation is discarded.

  • The upstream media are cold: \(P_1 \simeq P_4 \simeq 0\).

Model Equations#

Taking \(a=R_{\rm rs}\) and \(b=R_{\rm fs}\) in the momentum equation and collapsing to the razor-thin limit, the geometric pressure integral is smaller than the boundary terms by a factor of order the fractional shell thickness and is neglected. Applying the Rankine–Hugoniot momentum condition at each shock front replaces the downstream flux-plus-pressure terms by upstream momentum fluxes, so that for cold upstreams,

\[\boxed{ \frac{d\Pi_{\rm sh}}{dt} = 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2 \rho_1(R_{\rm sh},t)\,u_1(R_{\rm sh},t)\bigl[u_1(R_{\rm sh},t)-v_{\rm sh}\bigr] - 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2 \rho_4(R_{\rm sh},t)\,u_4(R_{\rm sh},t)\bigl[v_{\rm sh}-u_4(R_{\rm sh},t)\bigr]. }\]

The mass-loading rates at each shock are

\[\dot M_1 = 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2 \rho_1(R_{\rm sh},t) \left[ u_1(R_{\rm sh},t)-v_{\rm sh} \right], \qquad \dot M_4 = 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2 \rho_4(R_{\rm sh},t) \left[ v_{\rm sh}-u_4(R_{\rm sh},t) \right].\]

The complete conservative snowplow system is

\[\begin{split}\boxed{ \begin{aligned} \frac{dR_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= v_{\rm sh}, \\[4pt] \frac{dM_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= \dot M_1+\dot M_4, \\[4pt] \frac{d\Pi_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= u_{1,\rm sh}\dot M_1 + u_{4,\rm sh}\dot M_4, \\[4pt] v_{\rm sh} &= \frac{\Pi_{\rm sh}}{M_{\rm sh}}, \end{aligned} }\end{split}\]

where \(u_{1,\rm sh}=u_1(R_{\rm sh},t)\) and \(u_{4,\rm sh}=u_4(R_{\rm sh},t)\).

Derivation: acceleration form

Since \(\Pi_{\rm sh}=M_{\rm sh}v_{\rm sh}\), the product rule gives

\[\frac{d\Pi_{\rm sh}}{dt} = M_{\rm sh}\frac{dv_{\rm sh}}{dt} + v_{\rm sh}\frac{dM_{\rm sh}}{dt}.\]

Substituting the mass-loading rates,

\[M_{\rm sh}\frac{dv_{\rm sh}}{dt} = (u_{1,\rm sh}-v_{\rm sh})\dot M_1 + (u_{4,\rm sh}-v_{\rm sh})\dot M_4.\]

Expanding,

\[\boxed{ M_{\rm sh}\frac{dv_{\rm sh}}{dt} = 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2 \left[ \rho_{1,\rm sh} (u_{1,\rm sh}-v_{\rm sh})^2 - \rho_{4,\rm sh} (v_{\rm sh}-u_{4,\rm sh})^2 \right]. }\]

This form makes the physics explicit: faster ejecta overtaking the shell from behind accelerates it, while swept-up CSM ahead of the shell decelerates it.

For homologous ejecta (\(u_1 = r/t\)) and stationary CSM (\(u_4 = 0\)), the system reduces to

\[\begin{split}\boxed{ \begin{aligned} \frac{dR_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= v_{\rm sh}, \\[4pt] \frac{dM_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2 \left[ \rho_1(R_{\rm sh},t) \left( \frac{R_{\rm sh}}{t}-v_{\rm sh} \right) + \rho_4(R_{\rm sh},t)\,v_{\rm sh} \right], \\[4pt] \frac{d\Pi_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2 \rho_1(R_{\rm sh},t) \frac{R_{\rm sh}}{t} \left( \frac{R_{\rm sh}}{t}-v_{\rm sh} \right), \\[4pt] v_{\rm sh} &= \frac{\Pi_{\rm sh}}{M_{\rm sh}}. \end{aligned} }\end{split}\]

Pressure-Driven Thin-Shell Model#

The pressure-driven thin-shell model (PressureDrivenThinShellShockEngine) also collapses the shocked interaction region into a single thin shell of mass \(M_{\rm sh}=M_2+M_3\), radius \(R_{\rm sh}\), and velocity \(v_{\rm sh}\). However, rather than driving the shell with advective momentum fluxes, it estimates the immediate post-shock pressures from the Rankine–Hugoniot jump conditions and uses their net difference to accelerate the shell.

This closure is intermediate in complexity: it incorporates pressure physics without requiring the evolution of separate internal-energy variables for each shocked layer. It is particularly well suited to scenarios where one wants a pressure-balance approximation without the additional overhead of the full mechanical model.

Assumptions#

  • The shocked region collapses to a thin shell at \(R_{\rm sh}\), so that \(R_{\rm rs}\simeq R_{\rm cd}\simeq R_{\rm fs}\equiv R_{\rm sh}\).

  • The shell is accelerated by the pressure difference \(P_2 - P_3\) across the contact discontinuity.

  • The pressures \(P_2\) and \(P_3\) are evaluated algebraically from the instantaneous Rankine–Hugoniot jump conditions; no internal energy is evolved.

  • The upstream media are cold: \(P_1 \simeq P_4 \simeq 0\).

Important

The pressures \(P_2\) and \(P_3\) in this model are instantaneous algebraic functions of the upstream state and the shell velocity — they are not evolved as thermodynamic quantities.

Model Equations#

The shell acceleration is closed by the net post-shock pressure force,

\[\boxed{ M_{\rm sh} \frac{dv_{\rm sh}}{dt} = 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2 \left(P_2-P_3\right). }\]

For a cold, strong, non-relativistic shock with compression ratio \(\chi = (\hat\gamma+1)/(\hat\gamma-1)\), the downstream pressure is

\[P_d = \rho_u v_{\rm rel}^2 \left( 1-\frac{1}{\chi} \right).\]

Defining the ejecta–shell velocity lag \(\Delta \equiv u_1(R_{\rm sh},t) - v_{\rm sh}\), the post-shock pressures are

\[\boxed{ P_2 = \rho_1(R_{\rm sh},t) \,\Delta^2 \left( 1-\frac{1}{\chi} \right), \qquad P_3 = \rho_4(R_{\rm sh},t) \left[ v_{\rm sh}-u_4(R_{\rm sh},t) \right]^2 \left( 1-\frac{1}{\chi} \right). }\]

The shell mass evolves through mass loading at both shock fronts:

\[\frac{dM_2}{dt} = 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2\, \rho_1(R_{\rm sh},t)\,\Delta, \qquad \frac{dM_3}{dt} = 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2\, \rho_4(R_{\rm sh},t) \left[ v_{\rm sh}-u_4(R_{\rm sh},t) \right].\]

The complete pressure-driven thin-shell system is

\[\begin{split}\boxed{ \begin{aligned} \frac{dR_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= v_{\rm sh}, \\[6pt] \frac{dv_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= \frac{4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2}{M_{\rm sh}} \left( 1-\frac{1}{\chi} \right) \left[ \rho_1(R_{\rm sh},t)\,\Delta^2 - \rho_4(R_{\rm sh},t) \left(v_{\rm sh}-u_4(R_{\rm sh},t)\right)^2 \right], \\[6pt] \frac{dM_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2 \left[ \rho_1(R_{\rm sh},t)\,\Delta + \rho_4(R_{\rm sh},t) \left(v_{\rm sh}-u_4(R_{\rm sh},t)\right) \right], \end{aligned} }\end{split}\]

where \(\Delta \equiv u_1(R_{\rm sh},t) - v_{\rm sh}\).

Derivation: post-shock pressure from Rankine–Hugoniot

In the shock rest frame, momentum conservation across a non-relativistic shock is

\[P_u+\rho_u v_u^2 = P_d+\rho_d v_d^2.\]

For a cold upstream (\(P_u\simeq 0\)) with compression ratio \(\chi=\rho_d/\rho_u\), mass conservation gives \(v_d = v_u/\chi\), so

\[\rho_u v_u^2 = P_d+\rho_u\frac{v_u^2}{\chi}.\]

Hence

\[P_d = \rho_u v_u^2 \left(1-\frac{1}{\chi}\right).\]

For the reverse shock, \(v_u=\Delta\equiv u_1(R_{\rm sh},t)-v_{\rm sh}\); for the forward shock, \(v_u=v_{\rm sh}-u_4(R_{\rm sh},t)\).

For homologous ejecta (\(u_1 = r/t\)) and stationary CSM (\(u_4 = 0\)), the velocity lag simplifies to \(\Delta = R_{\rm sh}/t - v_{\rm sh}\) and the system reduces to

\[\begin{split}\boxed{ \begin{aligned} \frac{dR_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= v_{\rm sh}, \\[6pt] \frac{dv_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= \frac{4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2}{M_{\rm sh}} \left( 1-\frac{1}{\chi} \right) \left[ \rho_1(R_{\rm sh},t)\,\Delta^2 - \rho_4(R_{\rm sh},t)\,v_{\rm sh}^2 \right], \\[6pt] \frac{dM_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2 \left[ \rho_1(R_{\rm sh},t)\,\Delta + \rho_4(R_{\rm sh},t)\,v_{\rm sh} \right], \end{aligned} }\end{split}\]

where \(\Delta = R_{\rm sh}/t - v_{\rm sh}\).

Mechanical Internal-Energy Model#

The mechanical model (MechanicalShockEngine) is the most physically complete of the three numerical closures. Rather than collapsing the shocked region into a single shell, it assigns separate masses, internal energies, and effective volumes to the shocked ejecta (Region 2) and shocked CSM (Region 3), and evolves all of them as coupled ODEs.

This closure is appropriate when the shocked gas stores thermal energy that continues to provide pressure support over dynamical timescales — the regime relevant to adiabatic or partially radiative shocks early in the evolution of a remnant.

Assumptions#

  • The contact discontinuity at \(R_{\rm cd}\) separates two finite-width shocked layers:

    \[R_{\rm rs} = R_{\rm cd} - \Delta_2, \qquad R_{\rm fs} = R_{\rm cd} + \Delta_3.\]

  • The shock speeds are \(D_{\rm rs} = v_{\rm cd} - \dot\Delta_2\) and \(D_{\rm fs} = v_{\rm cd} + \dot\Delta_3\).

  • The volumes of the shocked layers are approximated by thin-slab expressions,

    \[V_2 \simeq 4\pi R_{\rm cd}^2\Delta_2, \qquad V_3 \simeq 4\pi R_{\rm cd}^2\Delta_3.\]

  • The layer-averaged pressures are thermodynamic functions of the evolved internal energies:

    \[P_i = (\hat\gamma_i-1)\frac{U_i}{V_i}.\]

  • The contact discontinuity is accelerated by the net pressure force with effective mass \(M_{\rm eff}=M_2+M_3\).

  • The upstream media are cold: \(P_1\simeq P_4\simeq 0\).

Important

In this model, \(P_2\) and \(P_3\) are not instantaneous Rankine–Hugoniot pressures. They are layer-averaged pressures obtained from the internal-energy budget of each shocked region, and they evolve continuously as mass is swept up and work is done by expansion.

Model Equations#

The full state vector is

\[{\bf y} = \left( R_{\rm cd},\; v_{\rm cd},\; M_2,\; M_3,\; U_2,\; U_3,\; \Delta_2,\; \Delta_3 \right).\]

The mass-loading equations are

\[\boxed{ \frac{dM_2}{dt} = 4\pi R_{\rm rs}^2 \rho_1(R_{\rm rs},t) \left[ u_1(R_{\rm rs},t)-D_{\rm rs} \right], \qquad \frac{dM_3}{dt} = 4\pi R_{\rm fs}^2 \rho_4(R_{\rm fs},t) \left[ D_{\rm fs}-u_4(R_{\rm fs},t) \right]. }\]

The contact-discontinuity equation of motion is

\[\boxed{ (M_2+M_3) \frac{dv_{\rm cd}}{dt} = 4\pi R_{\rm cd}^2(P_2-P_3). }\]

The internal energies evolve according to

\[\boxed{ \frac{dU_i}{dt} = \dot U_{{\rm sh},i} + \dot U_{{\rm ad},i} + \dot U_{{\rm rad},i}, \qquad i\in\{2,3\}, }\]

with the shock-heating term

\[\boxed{ \dot U_{{\rm sh},i} = \frac{1}{\hat\gamma_i-1} \left( \frac{\chi_i-1}{\chi_i^2} \right) v_{{\rm rel},i}^2 \frac{dM_i}{dt}, }\]

where \(v_{{\rm rel},2}=u_1(R_{\rm rs},t)-D_{\rm rs}\) and \(v_{{\rm rel},3}=D_{\rm fs}-u_4(R_{\rm fs},t)\), the adiabatic expansion term

\[\boxed{ \dot U_{{\rm ad},i} = -(\hat\gamma_i-1)U_i \left( 2\frac{v_{\rm cd}}{R_{\rm cd}} + \frac{\dot\Delta_i}{\Delta_i} \right), }\]

and \(\dot U_{{\rm rad},i}\) an external radiative loss to be specified by the user. The layer widths are closed by the layer sound speed,

\[\boxed{ \dot\Delta_i\simeq c_{s,i}, \qquad c_{s,i}^2 \simeq \hat\gamma_i\frac{P_i V_i}{M_i}. }\]

Collecting all equations, the minimal mechanical model is

\[\begin{split}\boxed{ \begin{aligned} \frac{dR_{\rm cd}}{dt} &= v_{\rm cd}, \\[6pt] \frac{dM_2}{dt} &= 4\pi R_{\rm rs}^2 \rho_1(R_{\rm rs},t) \left[ u_1(R_{\rm rs},t)-D_{\rm rs} \right], \\[6pt] \frac{dM_3}{dt} &= 4\pi R_{\rm fs}^2 \rho_4(R_{\rm fs},t) \left[ D_{\rm fs}-u_4(R_{\rm fs},t) \right], \\[6pt] (M_2+M_3) \frac{dv_{\rm cd}}{dt} &= 4\pi R_{\rm cd}^2(P_2-P_3), \\[6pt] \frac{dU_i}{dt} &= \dot U_{{\rm sh},i} + \dot U_{{\rm ad},i} + \dot U_{{\rm rad},i}, \\[6pt] \dot\Delta_i &\simeq c_{s,i}. \end{aligned} }\end{split}\]
Derivation: shock-heating source term

For a cold, strong, non-relativistic shock, the compression ratio is

\[\chi_i = \frac{\hat\gamma_i+1}{\hat\gamma_i-1}.\]

The downstream pressure generated by upstream relative velocity \(v_{\rm rel}\) is

\[P_d = \rho_u v_{\rm rel}^2 \left(1-\frac{1}{\chi_i}\right).\]

Since \(\rho_d=\chi_i\rho_u\), the downstream specific internal energy is

\[\varepsilon_{{\rm int},d} = \frac{1}{\hat\gamma_i-1} \frac{P_d}{\rho_d} = \frac{1}{\hat\gamma_i-1} \left( \frac{\chi_i-1}{\chi_i^2} \right) v_{\rm rel}^2.\]

Multiplying by the mass-loading rate gives the shock-heating source \(\dot U_{{\rm sh},i} = \varepsilon_{{\rm int},i}\,dM_i/dt\).

Derivation: adiabatic energy loss

The adiabatic term follows from \(P\,dV\) work:

\[\dot U_{{\rm ad},i} = -P_i\frac{dV_i}{dt} = -(\hat\gamma_i-1)U_i \frac{d\ln V_i}{dt}.\]

With \(V_i\simeq4\pi R_{\rm cd}^2\Delta_i\),

\[\frac{d\ln V_i}{dt} = 2\frac{v_{\rm cd}}{R_{\rm cd}} + \frac{\dot\Delta_i}{\Delta_i},\]

giving the boxed expression for \(\dot U_{{\rm ad},i}\).

Relativistic Models#

We now generalize to the relativistic regime. The geometry remains the standard four-region structure, but the dynamics and shock jump conditions are governed by special relativity. The upstream media are specified by their comoving rest-mass densities and lab-frame velocities:

\[\rho_1(r,t),\;\beta_1(r,t) \qquad\text{(unshocked ejecta)},\]
\[\rho_4(r,t),\;\beta_4(r,t) \qquad\text{(unshocked CSM)}.\]

The lab-frame Lorentz factors are \(\Gamma_i = 1/\!\sqrt{1-\beta_i^2}\). At the shell radius we write \(\rho_{i,\rm sh}\equiv\rho_i(R_{\rm sh},t)\), \(\beta_{i,\rm sh}\equiv\beta_i(R_{\rm sh},t)\), and \(\Gamma_{i,\rm sh}\equiv(1-\beta_{i,\rm sh}^2)^{-1/2}\).

Relativistic Pressure-Driven Thin-Shell Model#

The relativistic pressure-driven thin-shell model (RelPressureDrivenThinShellShockEngine) is the relativistic analogue of the non-relativistic pressure-driven thin-shell model. The shocked region is collapsed to a geometrically thin shell at a single radius \(R_{\rm sh}\), and the shell dynamics are governed by the net post-shock pressure difference across the contact discontinuity, evaluated from the relativistic Rankine–Hugoniot jump conditions.

Assumptions#

  • Thin-shell geometry: \(R_{\rm rs}\simeq R_{\rm cd}\simeq R_{\rm fs}\equiv R_{\rm sh}\).

  • The shell moves at the contact-discontinuity speed: \(dR_{\rm sh}/dt = c\beta_{\rm sh}\).

  • Shell momentum is driven by the net pressure force \(4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2(P_2-P_3)\) across the contact discontinuity.

  • Upstream media are cold: \(P_1\simeq P_4\simeq 0\).

  • The model is adiabatic; no radiative losses are included.

Dynamical State#

In the relativistic case, shocked internal energy contributes to the shell inertia alongside the baryonic rest mass, so the shell is described by its lab-frame four-momentum. The dynamical state vector is

\[\boxed{ \mathbf{y}(t) = \bigl( R_{\rm sh},\; M_{\rm sh},\; E_{\rm sh},\; \Pi_{\rm sh} \bigr), }\]

where \(M_{\rm sh}\) is the swept baryonic rest mass, \(E_{\rm sh}\) is the lab-frame total energy, and \(\Pi_{\rm sh}\) is the lab-frame radial momentum of the shocked shell.

The shell velocity follows algebraically from the four-momentum ratio,

(1)#\[\boxed{ \beta_{\rm sh} = \frac{\Pi_{\rm sh}\,c}{E_{\rm sh}}, \qquad \Gamma_{\rm sh} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta_{\rm sh}^2}}, \qquad w_{\rm sh} = \Gamma_{\rm sh}\beta_{\rm sh}. }\]

Here \(w_{\rm sh} = \Gamma_{\rm sh}\beta_{\rm sh}\) is the proper velocity of the shell, the spatial component of the four-velocity. This parametrization remains well behaved as \(\beta_{\rm sh}\to 1\).

The shell-frame energy is the Lorentz-invariant norm of the four-momentum,

(2)#\[E'_{\rm sh} = \sqrt{E_{\rm sh}^2 - \Pi_{\rm sh}^2\,c^2},\]

so the effective inertial mass \(\mathcal{M}_{\rm sh}=E'_{\rm sh}/c^2\) needs no independent evolution.

Shock Closure and Jump Conditions#

In the thin-shell approximation, both shocks travel at the shell speed \(\beta_{\rm sh}\). The upstream speed in the shell (downstream) frame follows from relativistic velocity addition:

\[\beta_{\rm rel,2} = \left| \frac{\beta_{1,\rm sh}-\beta_{\rm sh}} {1-\beta_{1,\rm sh}\,\beta_{\rm sh}} \right|, \qquad \beta_{\rm rel,3} = \left| \frac{\beta_{\rm sh}-\beta_{4,\rm sh}} {1-\beta_{\rm sh}\,\beta_{4,\rm sh}} \right|,\]

with Lorentz factors \(\Gamma_{\rm rel,i}=(1-\beta_{\rm rel,i}^2)^{-1/2}\).

For a cold upstream and ideal-gas downstream with adiabatic index \(\hat\gamma\), the relativistic Rankine–Hugoniot conditions give the post-shock pressure and energy-loading coefficient:

\[P_d^{({\rm RH})} = \rho_u\,c^2 (\Gamma_{\rm rel}-1)(\hat\gamma\,\Gamma_{\rm rel}+1), \qquad \eta_d = \frac{e_d}{\rho_d\,c^2} = \Gamma_{\rm rel},\]

where \(e_d = \rho_d c^2 + u_d\) is the downstream comoving total energy density. The coefficient \(\eta_d c^2\) is the shell-frame energy injected per unit swept rest mass.

Derivation: relativistic Rankine–Hugoniot jump conditions

For a cold upstream (\(P_u=0\), \(e_u=\rho_u c^2\)) and a relativistic ideal gas downstream, the Taub adiabat and the Rankine–Hugoniot conservation laws give (in the shock rest frame, with upstream Lorentz factor \(\Gamma_{\rm rel}\)):

Compression ratio:

\[\frac{\rho_d}{\rho_u} = \frac{\hat\gamma\Gamma_{\rm rel}+1}{\hat\gamma-1}.\]

Downstream pressure:

\[P_d = \rho_u c^2(\Gamma_{\rm rel}-1)(\hat\gamma\Gamma_{\rm rel}+1).\]

Energy-loading coefficient \(\eta_d\): using \(e_d = \rho_d c^2 + P_d/(\hat\gamma-1)\) and the compression ratio,

\[\eta_d = \frac{e_d}{\rho_d c^2} = 1 + \frac{P_d}{(\hat\gamma-1)\,\rho_d c^2} = 1 + \frac{(\Gamma_{\rm rel}-1)(\hat\gamma\Gamma_{\rm rel}+1)} {(\hat\gamma\Gamma_{\rm rel}+1)} = \Gamma_{\rm rel}.\]

Numerical Implementation: Pre-tabulation and Exact Pressure Closure#

The engine does not evaluate the ODE pressures directly from \(\Gamma_{\rm rel,i}\). Instead it uses the exact Rankine–Hugoniot result derived by combining baryon conservation, energy conservation, and momentum conservation in the shock frame.

Pre-tabulation. At initialisation the engine solves the full cold-upstream relativistic implicit equation

\[\left(\frac{\hat\gamma-1}{\hat\gamma}\right) \frac{\Gamma_{1s}\beta_{1s}}{\Gamma_{2s}\beta_{2s}} \left[\frac{\Gamma_{1s}}{\Gamma_{2s}} - 1\right] = \Gamma_{1s}^2\beta_{1s}^2 \left(1 - \frac{\beta_{2s}}{\beta_{1s}}\right)\]

numerically for \(\beta_{2s}(\beta_{1s})\) at each node of a fine \(\beta_{1s}\) grid. Separate tables are built for the adiabatic indices \(\hat\gamma_1\) (reverse shock) and \(\hat\gamma_4\) (forward shock). During ODE integration the downstream shock-frame speeds \(\beta_{2s}\) and \(\beta_{3s}\) are obtained by linear interpolation from these tables, avoiding a root-find at every function evaluation.

Post-shock quantities. Given the upstream shock-frame speed \(\beta_{is}\) and the interpolated downstream speed \(\beta_{ds}\) at each face, the post-shock state used in the ODE is:

Compression ratio (baryon-number conservation in the shock frame):

\[R_i = \frac{\rho_{d,i}}{\rho_{u,i}} = \frac{\Gamma_{is}\,\beta_{is}}{\Gamma_{ds}\,\beta_{ds}}.\]

Post-shock density:

\[\rho_{d,i} = R_i\,\rho_{u,i}.\]

Post-shock pressure (combining energy and momentum conservation in the shock frame for a cold upstream \(P_u=0\)):

\[\boxed{ P_i = \rho_{u,i}\,c^2\,\Gamma_{is}^2\,\beta_{is}^2 \!\left(1 - \frac{\beta_{ds}}{\beta_{is}}\right). }\]

This is the exact result from the relativistic jump conditions; no additional correction factor is required. In the non-relativistic limit \(\beta_{is}\ll 1\) it reduces to the correct strong-shock Hugoniot pressure \((1-1/\chi)\,\rho_u v_{\rm rel}^2\).

Derivation: exact pressure from conservation laws

In the shock rest frame, for a cold upstream (\(P_u=0\), \(e_u=\rho_u c^2\)), the three conservation conditions are:

Baryon conservation (mass flux):

\[\rho_u\,\Gamma_{us}\,\beta_{us} = \rho_d\,\Gamma_{ds}\,\beta_{ds}.\]

Energy conservation (energy flux):

\[\rho_u\,c^2\,\Gamma_{us}^2\,\beta_{us} = (e_d+P_d)\,\Gamma_{ds}^2\,\beta_{ds}.\]

Momentum conservation (momentum flux):

\[\rho_u\,c^2\,\Gamma_{us}^2\,\beta_{us}^2 = (e_d+P_d)\,\Gamma_{ds}^2\,\beta_{ds}^2 + P_d.\]

Dividing the energy equation by \(\beta_{us}\) gives

\[(e_d+P_d)\,\Gamma_{ds}^2\,\beta_{ds} = \rho_u\,c^2\,\Gamma_{us}^2\,\beta_{us}.\]

Substituting into the momentum equation:

\[\rho_u\,c^2\,\Gamma_{us}^2\,\beta_{us}^2 = \rho_u\,c^2\,\Gamma_{us}^2\,\beta_{us}\,\beta_{ds} + P_d.\]

Rearranging gives the boxed formula:

\[P_d = \rho_u\,c^2\,\Gamma_{us}^2\,\beta_{us}^2 \!\left(1-\frac{\beta_{ds}}{\beta_{us}}\right).\]

Energy-loading coefficient (from the ideal-gas equation of state applied to the reconstructed downstream state):

\[\eta_i = 1 + \frac{P_i}{(\hat\gamma-1)\,\rho_{d,i}\,c^2}.\]

When \(\beta_{ds}\) is the exact solution of the implicit equation, \(\eta_i\) is numerically equal to \(\Gamma_{\rm rel,i}\) from the standard jump-condition result.

Note

The diagnostic pressures stored in RelThinShellShockState are the simplified Hugoniot pressures \(P_d^{({\rm RH})}=\rho_u\,c^2(\Gamma_{\rm rel}-1)(\hat\gamma\,\Gamma_{\rm rel}+1)\), evaluated with \(\Gamma_{\rm rel}\) from the lab-frame velocity-addition formula. The ODE integration uses the exact momentum-flux pressures \(P_i\) defined above.

Rest-Mass Fluxes#

The baryonic rest mass swept into the shell per unit time follows from the comoving-frame baryon-flux condition at each shock face:

(3)#\[\boxed{ \dot{M}_2 = 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2\,\rho_{1,\rm sh}\,\Gamma_{1,\rm sh}\,c \left(\beta_{1,\rm sh}-\beta_{\rm sh}\right), }\]
(4)#\[\boxed{ \dot{M}_3 = 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2\,\rho_{4,\rm sh}\,\Gamma_{4,\rm sh}\,c \left(\beta_{\rm sh}-\beta_{4,\rm sh}\right), }\]

clamped to be non-negative.

Equations of Motion#

Combining baryon conservation, energy loading, and the pressure-driven momentum source gives the closed adiabatic system:

(5)#\[\begin{split}\boxed{ \begin{aligned} \frac{dR_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= c\,\beta_{\rm sh}, \\[6pt] \frac{dM_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= \dot{M}_2+\dot{M}_3, \\[6pt] \frac{dE_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= \Gamma_{\rm sh}\,c^2 \!\left( \Gamma_{\rm rel,2}\,\dot{M}_2 + \Gamma_{\rm rel,3}\,\dot{M}_3 \right), \\[6pt] \frac{d\Pi_{\rm sh}}{dt} &= w_{\rm sh}\,c \!\left( \Gamma_{\rm rel,2}\,\dot{M}_2 + \Gamma_{\rm rel,3}\,\dot{M}_3 \right) + 4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2 \!\left(P_2-P_3\right), \\[6pt] \beta_{\rm sh} &= \frac{\Pi_{\rm sh}\,c}{E_{\rm sh}}. \end{aligned} }\end{split}\]

The \(\Gamma_{\rm rel,i}\) factors in the energy and momentum equations are the energy-loading coefficients \(\eta_i=\Gamma_{\rm rel,i}\) from the Rankine–Hugoniot solution. The pressures \(P_2\) and \(P_3\) are the exact momentum-flux pressures defined in the pre-tabulation section above.

Derivation: energy and momentum loading

When newly shocked material is incorporated into the thin shell, it is boosted from the upstream frame to the shell rest frame, depositing shell-frame energy at the rate

\[\dot{E}'_{\rm load} = c^2 \!\left( \eta_2\,\dot{M}_2 + \eta_3\,\dot{M}_3 \right) = c^2 \!\left( \Gamma_{\rm rel,2}\,\dot{M}_2 + \Gamma_{\rm rel,3}\,\dot{M}_3 \right).\]

Since the newly shocked material is at rest in the shell frame, its contribution to the lab-frame four-momentum is parallel to the shell four-velocity \(U^\mu_{\rm sh}\propto(\Gamma_{\rm sh},\,w_{\rm sh})\). Boosting to the lab frame:

\[\dot{E}_{\rm load} = \Gamma_{\rm sh}\,\dot{E}'_{\rm load}, \qquad \dot{\Pi}_{\rm load} = \frac{w_{\rm sh}}{c}\,\dot{E}'_{\rm load},\]

which are the source terms appearing in the ODE system above.

In the non-relativistic limit \(w_{\rm sh}\,c\to v_{\rm sh}\) and \(\Gamma_{\rm rel,i}\to 1\), giving \(\dot{\Pi}_{\rm load}\to v_{\rm sh}(\dot{M}_2+\dot{M}_3)\) — the familiar variable-mass momentum term.

The pressure-driven term \(4\pi R_{\rm sh}^2(P_2-P_3)\) is the net relativistic pressure force on the contact discontinuity. It is not added on top of a snowplow momentum-flux term; it is the sole dynamical source beyond mass loading, defining the pressure-driven approximation.

Initial Conditions#

The initial state is specified by the triple \((R_0,\,\beta_0,\,M_0)\) at time \(t_0\). Assuming a cold shell,

\[E_0 = M_0\,c^2\,\Gamma_0, \qquad \Pi_0 = M_0\,c\,\Gamma_0\,\beta_0, \qquad \Gamma_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta_0^2}}.\]
