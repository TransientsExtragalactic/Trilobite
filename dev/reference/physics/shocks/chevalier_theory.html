Theory: The Chevalier Self-Similar Solution#

The Chevalier self-similar solution (ChevalierSelfSimilarShockEngine) provides a physically motivated description of shocks generated by supernovae and other explosive transients interacting with a surrounding circumstellar medium (CSM). Originally derived by Chevalier[1] to model radio and X-ray emission from supernovae, this solution has since become the foundational framework for shock-powered emission modeling across a broad range of astrophysical transients.

Overview and Assumptions#

The Chevalier solution describes the interaction between homologously expanding supernova ejecta and an ambient medium under the assumption of self-similar evolution.

Model Assumptions

  • The ejecta density follows a power-law in velocity space:

    \[\rho(r, t) = A_1\, t^{-3} \left(\frac{r}{t}\right)^{-n}\]

  • The CSM density follows a power-law in radius:

    \[\rho_{\rm csm}(r) = A_4\, r^{-s}\]

  • Self-similar dynamics — the shock radius and velocity evolve as power laws in time; there is no intrinsic length or timescale.

  • Strong, adiabatic shocks — Rankine–Hugoniot jump conditions relate upstream and downstream thermodynamic quantities.

  • Thin-shell approximation — the forward and reverse shocks are sufficiently close that the shocked region may be treated as a single thin shell when computing radiative properties. While Chevalier[1] solved the full self-similar ODE system for the detailed internal structure, the thin-shell approximation (following Chevalier[2]) has since become standard practice for radiative modeling.

Self-Similar Scaling Relations#

In a self-similar framework, there is no intrinsic length or timescale, so all ratios of physical quantities must remain constant over time. Fixing the ratio of ejecta to CSM density at the shock radius constrains the time evolution of the shock:

Key Result: Shock Scaling

(1)#\[R_{\rm sh}(t) = \left[\frac{A_4}{A_1}\,\zeta\right]^{1/(s-n)} t^{(3-n)/(s-n)} \equiv A_{\rm sh}\, t^{\lambda},\]

where \(\zeta\) is a dimensionless constant set by the solution, and

\[\lambda = \frac{n-3}{n-s}.\]

The shock velocity follows immediately:

(2)#\[v_{\rm sh}(t) = \frac{dR_{\rm sh}}{dt} = \lambda\,\frac{R_{\rm sh}}{t}.\]

The normalization constant \(A_{\rm sh}\) encodes the detailed internal structure of the shocked region and cannot, in general, be obtained in closed form. This motivates the thin-shell approximation, which permits an analytic expression for \(\zeta\).

Thin-Shell Normalization#

Key Result: Normalization Constant

\[\zeta = \frac{ 3\lambda^2 + 4\,\dfrac{(\lambda - 1)\lambda}{3-s} }{ 3(1-\lambda)^2 - 4\,\dfrac{(\lambda - 1)\lambda}{n-3} }\]

For a wind-like CSM (\(s = 2\)) this simplifies to

\[\zeta = \frac{(n-4)(n-3)}{2},\]

consistent with Chevalier[2]. With \(\zeta\) in hand, the full shock dynamics are determined.

Density Normalizations#

Ejecta Density#

To express \(A_1\) in terms of observable parameters, we adopt a broken power-law ejecta profile with inner slope \(\delta\) and outer slope \(n\), motivated by hydrodynamical simulations:

\[\begin{split}\rho(r,t) = K\,t^{-3} \begin{cases} v^{-\delta}, & v < v_t, \\ v_t^{n-\delta}\,v^{-n}, & v \geq v_t. \end{cases}\end{split}\]

Key Result: Ejecta Normalization

In terms of total ejecta mass \(M_{\rm ej}\) and kinetic energy \(E_{\rm ej}\):

\[v_t^2 = \frac{2(5-\delta)(n-5)}{(3-\delta)(n-3)} \frac{E_{\rm ej}}{M_{\rm ej}},\]
\[K = \frac{1}{4\pi} \left(\frac{(3-\delta)(n-3)}{n-\delta}\right) \frac{M_{\rm ej}}{v_t^{3-\delta}}.\]

CSM Density#

The CSM normalization \(A_4\) may be specified directly or related to a fiducial density via

\[\rho_{\rm CSM}(r) = \rho_0 \left(\frac{r}{r_0}\right)^{-s} = A_4\,r^{-s}.\]

Wind-like CSM (\(s = 2\))

For a steady progenitor wind, mass conservation fixes \(A_4\):

\[A_4 = \frac{\dot{M}}{4\pi v_w},\]

where \(\dot{M}\) is the progenitor mass-loss rate and \(v_w\) is the wind velocity. This is the most widely used normalization for modeling CSM around massive stars that underwent steady mass loss prior to explosion.

Two-Shock Self-Similar Structure#

The thin-shell model described above is useful because it gives an analytic radius normalization and a fast estimate of the contact-discontinuity dynamics. However, the original Chevalier solution[1] is not a literal single-shell model. It resolves the shocked interaction region into two self-similar layers separated by a contact discontinuity:

  • Region 2: shocked ejecta, bounded by the reverse shock and the contact discontinuity.

  • Region 3: shocked CSM, bounded by the contact discontinuity and the forward shock.

The two-shock solver implemented in Trilobite follows this more complete structure. Instead of approximating the shocked region as a single thin shell, it solves the self-similar hydrodynamic equations on both sides of the contact discontinuity and determines the dimensionless constants

\[A,\qquad \frac{R_{\rm fs}}{R_c},\qquad \frac{R_{\rm rs}}{R_c},\]

where \(R_c\) is the contact-discontinuity radius, \(R_{\rm fs}\) is the forward-shock radius, and \(R_{\rm rs}\) is the reverse-shock radius.

The full time-dependent kinematics then follow from

\[R_c(t) = \left[ A\,\frac{A_1}{A_4} \right]^{1/(n-s)} t^{(n-3)/(n-s)},\]

where \(A_1\) is the outer ejecta density normalization and \(A_4\) is the CSM density normalization. The forward and reverse shock radii are fixed multiples of the contact radius:

\[R_{\rm fs}(t) = \left(\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{R_c}\right)R_c(t), \qquad R_{\rm rs}(t) = \left(\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{R_c}\right)R_c(t).\]

Since all three surfaces evolve with the same power-law index, their velocities are

\[v_c = m\,\frac{R_c}{t}, \qquad v_{\rm fs} = m\,\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{t}, \qquad v_{\rm rs} = m\,\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{t},\]

where

\[m = \frac{n-3}{n-s}.\]

Note

The original Chevalier paper uses the reciprocal exponent

\[\Lambda = \frac{1}{m} = \frac{n-s}{n-3}.\]

In the code, expansion_index refers to \(m\), while similarity_exponent refers to \(\Lambda\).

Similarity Variables#

The two-sided solver uses a common similarity form for both shocked regions. On each side we define a local shock-normalized coordinate

\[x = \frac{r}{R_{\rm sh}(t)},\]

where \(R_{\rm sh}=R_{\rm fs}\) for the outer shocked CSM solution and \(R_{\rm sh}=R_{\rm rs}\) for the inner shocked ejecta solution. The shock is always located at \(x=1\).

The velocity, density, and pressure are written as

\[u(r,t) = \frac{r}{t}U(x),\]
\[\rho(r,t) = \rho_0\,t^a r^b \Omega(x),\]
\[p(r,t) = \rho_0\,t^{a-2}r^{b+2}P(x).\]

The powers \(a\) and \(b\) depend on which side is being solved.

For the outer shocked CSM,

\[\rho_{\rm CSM} = A_4 r^{-s}, \qquad a=0, \qquad b=-s, \qquad \rho_0=A_4.\]

For the inner shocked ejecta,

\[\rho_{\rm ej} = A_1 t^{n-3}r^{-n}, \qquad a=n-3, \qquad b=-n, \qquad \rho_0=A_1.\]

The dimensionless sound speed is

\[C^2 = \frac{\gamma P}{\Omega}.\]

The contact discontinuity is identified by the condition that the fluid velocity matches the contact velocity. Since

\[v_c = m\,\frac{R_c}{t},\]

and \(u=(r/t)U\), the contact condition is

\[U \rightarrow m.\]

This condition is approached from opposite sides in the two integrations. In the outer shocked CSM, the integration starts at the forward shock and proceeds inward until \(U \rightarrow m\). In the inner shocked ejecta, the integration starts at the reverse shock and proceeds outward until \(U \rightarrow m\).

Self-Similar ODE System#

Substituting the similarity ansatz into the spherical Euler equations gives a first-order ODE system for \(U(x)\), \(\Omega(x)\), and \(P(x)\). The solver uses the same algebraic form for both sides; only the powers \(a\) and \(b\) change.

Define

\[D = x(U-m).\]

Then the dimensionless equations are

\[D\frac{\Omega'}{\Omega} + xU' + a + (b+3)U = 0,\]
\[U^2-U + D U' = -\frac{(b+2)P+xP'}{\Omega},\]

and

\[D\left( \frac{P'}{P} - \gamma\frac{\Omega'}{\Omega} \right) + (a-2-\gamma a) + (b+2-\gamma b)U = 0.\]

Here primes denote derivatives with respect to \(x\).

For numerical stability, these equations are solved as a linear system for \(U'\) and \(P'\), after which \(\Omega'\) is recovered from the continuity equation. The matrix form is

\[\begin{split}\begin{pmatrix} D & x/\Omega \\ \gamma P x & D \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} U' \\ P' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} U-U^2-\dfrac{(b+2)P}{\Omega} \\ -P\left[ E+\gamma\left(a+(b+3)U\right) \right] \end{pmatrix},\end{split}\]

where

\[E = (a-2-\gamma a) + (b+2-\gamma b)U.\]

The density derivative is then

\[\Omega' = \Omega\, \frac{ -xU'-a-(b+3)U }{D}.\]

Shock Boundary Conditions#

The integrations begin from the immediate post-shock states. We assume strong, cold shocks with adiabatic index \(\gamma\).

Outer Forward Shock#

The outer shock propagates into stationary CSM. Its velocity is

\[V_{\rm fs} = m\,\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{t}.\]

For a strong shock into cold stationary material, the immediate downstream state is

\[u_1 = \frac{2}{\gamma+1}V_{\rm fs},\]
\[\rho_1 = \frac{\gamma+1}{\gamma-1}\rho_{\rm CSM}(R_{\rm fs}),\]
\[p_1 = \frac{2}{\gamma+1} \rho_{\rm CSM}(R_{\rm fs})V_{\rm fs}^2.\]

In dimensionless form this gives

\[U_1 = \frac{2m}{\gamma+1},\]
\[\Omega_1 = \frac{\gamma+1}{\gamma-1},\]
\[P_1 = \frac{2m^2}{\gamma+1}.\]

The outer integration starts at \(x=1\) and proceeds inward toward the contact discontinuity.

Inner Reverse Shock#

The reverse shock propagates into homologously expanding ejecta. The upstream ejecta velocity at the reverse shock is

\[u_{\rm ej}(R_{\rm rs},t) = \frac{R_{\rm rs}}{t},\]

while the reverse-shock speed is

\[V_{\rm rs} = m\,\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{t}.\]

The upstream ejecta therefore overtakes the reverse shock with relative speed

\[v_{\rm rel} = \frac{R_{\rm rs}}{t}-V_{\rm rs} = (1-m)\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{t}.\]

The immediate downstream state is

\[\rho_2 = \frac{\gamma+1}{\gamma-1}\rho_{\rm ej}(R_{\rm rs},t),\]
\[p_2 = \frac{2}{\gamma+1} \rho_{\rm ej}(R_{\rm rs},t)v_{\rm rel}^2,\]

and

\[u_2 = V_{\rm rs} + \frac{\gamma-1}{\gamma+1}v_{\rm rel}.\]

In dimensionless form,

\[U_2 = m + \frac{\gamma-1}{\gamma+1}(1-m),\]
\[\Omega_2 = \frac{\gamma+1}{\gamma-1},\]
\[P_2 = \frac{2(1-m)^2}{\gamma+1}.\]

For \(\gamma=5/3\), these reduce to the boundary conditions used by Chevalier[1],

\[U_2 = \frac{1}{4}\left(1+3m\right),\]
\[\Omega_2 = 4,\]
\[P_2 = \frac{3}{4}(1-m)^2.\]

The inner integration starts at \(x=1\) and proceeds outward toward the contact discontinuity.

Determining the Shock Radii#

Each side is solved in its own local shock-normalized coordinate \(x=r/R_{\rm sh}\). The contact discontinuity is located by the event

\[U(x_c) = m.\]

Since the shock is at \(x=1\), the contact location gives the shock radius relative to the contact radius:

\[\frac{R_{\rm sh}}{R_c} = \frac{1}{x_c}.\]

Therefore,

\[\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{R_c} = \frac{1}{x_{c,{\rm out}}}, \qquad \frac{R_{\rm rs}}{R_c} = \frac{1}{x_{c,{\rm in}}}.\]

In practice, the integration stops slightly before the formal contact singularity by using

\[U = m \mp \epsilon,\]

with the sign chosen according to which side is being integrated. This avoids evaluating exactly at the contact, where density or temperature may be singular even though pressure remains finite.

Pressure Matching and the Constant \(A\)#

The inner and outer solutions are joined by enforcing pressure continuity at the contact discontinuity. Define

\[C_1 = \left(\frac{p_c}{p_1}\right)_{\rm outer},\]

and

\[C_2 = \left(\frac{p_c}{p_2}\right)_{\rm inner},\]

where \(p_1\) and \(p_2\) are the immediate post-forward-shock and post-reverse-shock pressures, respectively.

Because the physical pressure contains an explicit radial factor,

\[p \propto r^{b+2}P(x),\]

the contact-to-shock pressure ratios are

\[C_1 = x_{c,{\rm out}}^{2-s} \frac{P(x_{c,{\rm out}})}{P_1},\]

for the outer shocked CSM, and

\[C_2 = x_{c,{\rm in}}^{2-n} \frac{P(x_{c,{\rm in}})}{P_2},\]

for the inner shocked ejecta.

Pressure continuity requires

\[C_1 p_1 = C_2 p_2.\]

Solving this condition for the radius normalization gives

\[A = \frac{C_2}{C_1} \left(\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{R_c}\right)^{2-n} \left(\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{R_c}\right)^{s-2} \left(\frac{1-m}{m}\right)^2.\]

Equivalently, since

\[\frac{1-m}{m} = \frac{3-s}{n-3},\]

we can write

\[A = \frac{C_2}{C_1} \left(\frac{R_{\rm rs}}{R_c}\right)^{2-n} \left(\frac{R_{\rm fs}}{R_c}\right)^{s-2} \left(\frac{3-s}{n-3}\right)^2.\]

This is the dimensionless constant used in the two-shock solver to normalize the contact radius.
