Theory Note: Stratified Synchrotron Absorption#

In certain scenarios, extreme cooling of the post-shock material results in a modification to the canonical synchrotron self-absorption (SSA) spectrum. This note outlines the theoretical basis for this effect, referred to as stratified synchrotron self-absorption, and derives the modified spectral scaling that arises in this regime. The classical paper on this effect is Granot et al.[1], which first introduced the concept in the context of gamma-ray burst afterglows. This effect is, generally, only relevant in relativistic shocks where post-shock fluid velocity allows for large spatial stratification of electron energies.

Standard Synchrotron Self-Absorption#

To introduce the notion of stratified synchrotron self-absorption (SSA), it is useful to first recount the standard argument for SSA in a homogeneous synchrotron-emitting plasma and to make explicit the assumptions underlying the familiar low-frequency behavior of the spectrum.

For an isotropic distribution of relativistic electrons with differential number density \(N(\gamma) \equiv d N / d\gamma\), the synchrotron self-absorption coefficient may be written (see, e.g., Rybicki and Lightman[2]) as

\[\alpha_\nu \propto \frac{1}{\nu^2} \int d\gamma\; P(\nu,\gamma)\, \gamma^2 \frac{d}{d\gamma} \!\left[ \frac{N(\gamma)}{\gamma^2} \right],\]

where \(P(\nu,\gamma)\) is the synchrotron power per unit frequency emitted by a single electron of Lorentz factor \(\gamma\).

At a fixed observing frequency \(\nu\), the structure of the synchrotron kernel implies that electrons with characteristic synchrotron frequency \(\nu_{\rm synch}(\gamma) \ll \nu\) lie on the exponentially suppressed high-frequency tail of \(P(\nu,\gamma)\) and therefore contribute negligibly to the absorption coefficient. As a result, the effective support of the integral is restricted to Lorentz factors

\[\gamma \gtrsim \gamma_{\rm synch}(\nu), \qquad \nu_{\rm synch}\!\left(\gamma_{\rm synch}(\nu)\right) \sim \nu,\]

and the absorption coefficient may be written schematically as

\[\alpha_\nu \propto \frac{1}{\nu^2} \int_{\gamma_{\rm synch}(\nu)}^{\infty} d\gamma\; P(\nu,\gamma)\, \gamma^2 \frac{d}{d\gamma} \!\left[ \frac{N(\gamma)}{\gamma^2} \right].\]

Note

The big idea here being that at each observing frequency, only electrons above a certain Lorentz factor contribute to absorption, because lower-energy electrons simply cannot absorb photons at that frequency (the synchrotron kernel is exponentially suppressed there). Likewise, it is the lowest-energy electrons above the cutoff that dominate absorption at low frequencies.

Now consider the regime in which the characteristic Lorentz factor \(\gamma_{\rm synch}(\nu)\) lies below the low-energy cutoff \(\gamma_m\) of the electron distribution, i.e.

\[\nu \ll \nu_m \equiv \nu_{\rm synch}(\gamma_m).\]

In this case, the only contribution comes from the low-energy tail of the synchrotron kernel, for which

\[P(\nu,\gamma) \propto \left(\frac{\nu}{\nu_{\rm synch}(\gamma)}\right)^{1/3} \propto \nu^{1/3}\,\gamma^{-2/3}.\]

Substituting this expression into the absorption coefficient yields the scaling

\[\alpha_\nu \propto \nu^{-5/3}\]

where the remaining integral is independent of \(\nu\). Thus, in a homogeneous source at frequencies well below \(\nu_m\), the absorption coefficient scales as \(\alpha_\nu \propto \nu^{-5/3}\).

In the same frequency regime, the synchrotron emissivity scales as \(j_\nu \propto \nu^{1/3}\), since both emission and absorption are controlled by electrons at the fixed Lorentz factor \(\gamma \simeq \gamma_m\). The resulting SSA source function,

\[S_\nu \equiv \frac{j_\nu}{\alpha_\nu},\]

therefore scales as

\[S_\nu \propto \nu^{2}.\]

In the optically thick limit (\(\alpha_\nu L \gg 1\), where \(L\) is the characteristic size of the emitting region), radiative transfer implies that the emergent specific intensity approaches the source function. For a source of fixed angular size, this yields the familiar low-frequency synchrotron self-absorbed spectrum,

\[F_\nu \propto \nu^{2}, \qquad \nu \ll \nu_m.\]

This conclusion relies critically on the assumption that the identity of the electrons controlling both emission and absorption is independent of frequency at sufficiently low \(\nu\).

In realistic astrophysical shocks, however—particularly in the fast-cooling regime—the post-shock plasma is not homogeneous. Electrons are continuously accelerated at the shock front and cool as they are advected downstream, producing a strong spatial stratification in the characteristic electron energy. As a result, the lowest-energy electrons capable of absorbing a given frequency depend on position within the flow. This stratification qualitatively modifies the optically thick synchrotron spectrum and leads to deviations from the standard \(F_\nu \propto \nu^2\) scaling, as discussed below.

Stratified SSA#

The primary difference between standard SSA and stratified SSA lies in the spatial variation of the electron energy distribution behind the shock front. In the stratified case, the electrons responsible for absorption at a given frequency vary with depth into the post-shock region, leading to a frequency-dependent SSA photosphere and a modified emergent spectrum. In this section, we’ll derive the relevant results and implications for the spectrum.

Post-Shock Stratification#

We begin by recognizing that, if cooling is sufficiently fast, then a significant degree of stratification develops immediately behind the shock front due to radiative losses. In each subsequent layer downstream, the characteristic electron energy decreases as electrons cool. This stratification affects both the emissivity and absorption coefficient as a function of depth behind the shock and significantly complicates the resulting emission.

Given a cooling process (e.g., synchrotron and/or inverse Compton) with rate function \(\Lambda\), the cooling timescale for an electron of Lorentz factor \(\gamma\) is

\[t_{\rm cool}(\gamma) = \frac{\gamma m_e c^2}{\Lambda(\gamma)}.\]

For electrons injected at the shock with a minimum Lorentz factor \(\gamma_m\), the cooling timescale \(t_{\rm cool}(\gamma_m)\) sets the thickness of the uncooled layer behind the shock:

\[\ell_{\rm uncooled} \sim v_{\rm ps}\,t_{\rm cool}(\gamma_m).\]

Likewise, for a given dynamical timescale \(t_{\rm dyn}\), one may define a cooling Lorentz factor

\[\gamma_c : t_{\rm cool}(\gamma_c) = t_{\rm dyn}.\]

and a corresponding cooling layer thickness

\[\ell_{\rm cool} \sim v_{\rm ps}\,t_{\rm dyn}.\]

The relative scales of these two lengths determine the degree of stratification in the post-shock region:

\[\boxed{ \frac{\ell_{\rm uncooled}}{\ell_{\rm cool}} = \frac{t_{\rm cool}(\gamma_m)}{t_{\rm dyn}} = \frac{\gamma_c}{\gamma_m}. }\]

In the fast-cooling regime (\(\gamma_c \ll \gamma_m\)), the uncooled layer is very thin (\(\ell_{\rm uncooled} \ll \ell_{\rm cool}\)), and most of the post-shock region is occupied by cooled electrons whose characteristic energy decreases with distance behind the shock. This leads to our big idea:

Big Idea

In the fast-cooling regime, the coldest electrons are located farthest from the shock front, meaning that the surface from which SSA emission escapes depends on frequency. For extremely low frequencies, even the uncooled layer is optically thick, leading to typical behavior, but at higher frequencies, the SSA photosphere recedes into progressively colder layers, modifying the emergent spectrum.

Radiative Transfer Through a Stratified Slab#

We now consider synchrotron radiation of a fixed observing frequency \(\nu\) emitted at some depth behind the shock and propagating outward toward the observer. Because electrons are injected only at the shock front and subsequently cool as they advect downstream, the post-shock region may be regarded as a stack of layers with monotonically increasing characteristic electron energy toward the shock.

It is therefore useful to describe the post-shock region in terms of two distinct slabs:

  1. an uncooled slab, extending from the shock front to depth \(\ell_{\rm uncooled} \sim v_{\rm ps} t_{\rm cool}(\gamma_m)\), in which electrons retain their injection energy \(\gamma \simeq \gamma_m\), and

  2. a cooled slab, extending from \(\ell_{\rm uncooled}\) to \(\ell_{\rm cool} \sim v_{\rm ps} t_{\rm dyn}\), in which the characteristic electron Lorentz factor decreases with depth due to radiative losses.

We adopt a coordinate \(x\) measured from the shock front downstream, such that \(x = 0\) corresponds to freshly shocked material.

Frequency-Dependent Absorption#

Consider radiation of frequency \(\nu\) propagating outward through successive layers at increasingly smaller \(x\). In a given layer at depth \(x\), the maximum electron Lorentz factor is set by cooling, \(\gamma_{\rm cool}(x)\), with an associated synchrotron frequency

\[\nu_{\rm cool}(x) \equiv \nu_{\rm synch}(\gamma_{\rm cool}(x)).\]

If \(\nu > \nu_{\rm cool}(x)\), then all electrons in that layer lie on the exponentially suppressed high-frequency tail of the synchrotron kernel. In this case, the local absorption coefficient is negligible:

\[\alpha_\nu(x) \simeq 0.\]

Such layers are effectively transparent at frequency \(\nu\).

Conversely, once the radiation reaches a layer for which

\[\nu \lesssim \nu_{\rm cool}(x),\]

electrons are present that can absorb efficiently at frequency \(\nu\). From this point toward the shock, the SSA optical depth begins to accumulate. We define \(x_k(\nu)\) as the first layer encountered (moving outward) for which this condition is satisfied.

The SSA photosphere at frequency \(\nu\) is then defined implicitly by

\[\tau_\nu(x_{\rm LS,\nu}) = \int_{x_k(\nu)}^{x_{\rm LS,\nu}} \alpha_\nu(x')\,dx' \simeq 1,\]

where \(x_{\rm LS,\nu}\) denotes the last-scattering surface for photons of frequency \(\nu\).

Absorption Coefficient in the Cooled Slab#

In the cooled slab, electrons have cooled down to the local Lorentz factor \(\gamma_{\rm cool}(x)\), and no electrons with \(\gamma > \gamma_{\rm cool}(x)\) are present. Absorption is therefore dominated by electrons near this cutoff.

Because absorption occurs only for electrons satisfying \(\nu \ll \nu_{\rm synch}(\gamma)\), the relevant part of the synchrotron kernel is its low-frequency asymptotic form,

\[P(\nu,\gamma) \propto \nu^{1/3}\,\gamma^{-2/3}.\]

Substituting this into the SSA coefficient yields the local scaling

\[\alpha_\nu(x) \propto \nu^{-5/3}\,\gamma_{\rm cool}(x)^{-5/3}.\]

In the fast-cooling regime, radiative losses imply

\[\gamma_{\rm cool}(x) \propto x^{-1},\]

so that

\[\alpha_\nu(x) \propto \nu^{-5/3}\,x^{5/3}.\]

Source Functions in the Stratified Medium#

A key simplification of the stratified SSA problem is that any layer which defines the SSA photosphere must lie on the Rayleigh–Jeans tail of the local synchrotron spectrum. Otherwise, the absorption coefficient would be exponentially suppressed and optical depth could not reach unity.

As a result, the local SSA source function always takes the Rayleigh–Jeans form,

\[S_\nu(x) = \frac{j_\nu(x)}{\alpha_\nu(x)} \propto \nu^2\,\gamma(x).\]

The distinction between the two slabs enters only through the depth-dependence of \(\gamma(x)\).

(1) Uncooled Slab#

In the uncooled slab (\(x \lesssim \ell_{\rm uncooled}\)), electrons retain their injection energy,

\[\gamma(x) \simeq \gamma_m = \text{constant}.\]

The local source function is therefore independent of depth:

\[S_\nu^{\rm (uncooled)} \propto \nu^2\,\gamma_m.\]

If the SSA photosphere lies within this slab, the emergent spectrum recovers the standard optically thick synchrotron scaling,

\[F_\nu \propto \nu^2.\]

(2) Cooled Slab#

In the cooled slab (\(x \gtrsim \ell_{\rm uncooled}\)), the characteristic electron energy decreases with depth,

\[\gamma(x) \propto x^{-1}.\]

As a result, the local SSA source function varies with depth as

\[S_\nu^{\rm (cooled)}(x) \propto \nu^2\,\gamma(x) \propto \nu^2\,x^{-1}.\]

To determine the emergent spectrum, we must therefore establish how the depth of the SSA photosphere depends on observing frequency.

As shown above, absorption at frequency \(\nu\) becomes effective only once the radiation reaches layers for which \(\nu \lesssim \nu_{\rm cool}(x)\). From that point inward toward the shock, the SSA optical depth accumulates according to

\[\tau_\nu(x) = \int_{x_k(\nu)}^{x} \alpha_\nu(x')\,dx',\]

where \(x_k(\nu)\) is the first layer capable of absorbing at frequency \(\nu\).

In the cooled slab, the local absorption coefficient scales as

\[\alpha_\nu(x) \propto \nu^{-5/3}\,\gamma_{\rm cool}(x)^{-5/3} \propto \nu^{-5/3}\,x^{5/3}.\]

Integrating this expression yields

\[\tau_\nu(x) \propto \nu^{-5/3} \int x'^{5/3}\,dx' \propto \nu^{-5/3}\,x^{8/3}.\]

The SSA photosphere \(x_{\rm LS,\nu}\) is defined by the condition \(\tau_\nu(x_{\rm LS,\nu}) \simeq 1\), implying

\[x_{\rm LS,\nu} \propto \nu^{-5/8}.\]

The observed intensity at frequency \(\nu\) is determined by the local source function evaluated at the SSA photosphere,

\[I_\nu \simeq S_\nu^{\rm (cooled)}\!\left(x_{\rm LS,\nu}\right).\]

Substituting the scalings derived above, we find

\[I_\nu \propto \nu^2\,x_{\rm LS,\nu}^{-1} \propto \nu^2\,\nu^{5/8} = \nu^{11/8}.\]

For a source of fixed angular size, the flux density therefore scales as

\[\boxed{ F_\nu \propto \nu^{11/8}, \qquad \nu_{\rm ac} < \nu < \nu_a. }\]

This modified optically thick slope arises entirely from the frequency dependence of the SSA photosphere depth in the cooling-stratified post-shock region. The local synchrotron source function remains Rayleigh–Jeans at all relevant depths; the deviation from the standard \(F_\nu \propto \nu^2\) behavior is a geometric consequence of stratification rather than a change in microphysics.

Spectral Breaks and Spectral Implications#

As we have shown previously, there are two distinct spectral regimes for stratified SSA determined by the location of the SSA photosphere relative to the uncooled and cooled slabs:

  • If the SSA photosphere lies within the uncooled slab (\(\nu < \nu_{\rm ac}\)), the emergent spectrum recovers the standard optically thick synchrotron scaling,

    \[F_\nu \propto \nu^{2}.\]

  • If the SSA photosphere lies within the cooled slab (\(\nu_{\rm ac} < \nu < \nu_a\)), the emergent spectrum exhibits the modified scaling derived above,

    \[F_\nu \propto \nu^{11/8}.\]
