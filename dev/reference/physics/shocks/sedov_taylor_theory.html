Theory: Sedov-Taylor Self-Similar Solution#

The Sedov-Taylor blast-wave solution (SedovTaylorShockEngine) describes the adiabatic expansion of a strong spherical shock driven by a point explosion into a uniform ambient medium. Originally derived independently by Sedov[1] and Taylor[2] in the context of nuclear blasts, the solution has since become a foundational framework for the adiabatic phase of supernova remnant evolution—the epoch during which the swept-up mass exceeds the initial ejecta mass but radiative losses remain unimportant.

This page derives the Sedov-Taylor scalings from first principles and documents in full the normalization procedure implemented in sedov_taylor_beta(), including the explicit parametric similarity profiles and the energy integral that fixes the normalization coefficient \(\beta(\gamma)\).

Overview#

We consider the idealized problem of an instantaneous explosion in a cold, uniform, initially static medium of density \(\rho_0\). The ambient pressure is taken to be negligible,

\[p_0 \simeq 0,\]

and the deposited energy \(E_0\) is assumed to be conserved during the phase of interest. In supernova remnants, this Sedov–Taylor phase typically begins after the freely expanding ejecta have swept up a mass comparable to their own and can last for hundreds to thousands of years before cooling becomes important.

The assumptions of the Sedov–Taylor problem are therefore:

  1. the explosion is effectively instantaneous;

  2. the ambient medium is uniform, cold, and initially at rest;

  3. the shock is strong, so the upstream Mach number is large;

  4. the evolution is adiabatic and energy conserving;

  5. gravity, radiative cooling, and continued energy injection are negligible.

We denote upstream, unshocked quantities by subscript \(0\) and downstream, post-shock quantities by subscript \(1\). The shock radius is \(R(t)\), and its velocity in the lab frame is

\[\dot{R} \equiv \frac{dR}{dt}.\]

Thin-Shell Estimate#

Before deriving the similarity solution, it is useful to obtain the basic scaling behavior using an approximate thin-shell argument. Since the shock is strong, the Rankine–Hugoniot jump conditions give the density contrast

\[\frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0} = \frac{\gamma + 1}{\gamma - 1},\]

where \(\gamma\) is the adiabatic index.

If the swept-up mass is concentrated into a thin shell of radius \(R\) and thickness \(D\), then mass conservation gives

\[4 \pi R^2 D \rho_1 = \frac{4\pi}{3} R^3 \rho_0.\]

Therefore,

\[D = \frac{R}{3}\frac{\rho_0}{\rho_1} = \frac{R}{3} \frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma + 1}.\]

Thus, for ordinary gases with \(\gamma\) of order unity, the shell is geometrically thin compared to its radius.

In the shock frame, the upstream gas flows into the shock with speed \(u_0\) and the downstream gas exits with speed \(u_1\). Mass conservation across the shock implies

\[\rho_0 u_0 = \rho_1 u_1,\]

so

\[u_1 = \frac{\rho_0}{\rho_1}u_0 = \frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma + 1}u_0.\]

The shock speed in the lab frame is the difference between the upstream and downstream velocities in the shock frame:

\[\dot{R} = u_0 - u_1 = \frac{2}{\gamma + 1}u_0.\]

Equivalently,

\[u_0 = \frac{\gamma + 1}{2}\dot{R}.\]

The momentum carried by the swept-up shell is approximately

\[p_{\rm shell} = \left(4\pi R^2D\right)\rho_1\dot{R} = \frac{4\pi}{3}R^3\rho_0\dot{R}.\]

Since the shell sweeps up more material as it expands, its momentum increases. This requires a pressure force from the hot interior. We parameterize the interior pressure by

\[P_{\rm in} = \alpha p_1,\]

where \(p_1\) is the immediate post-shock pressure and \(\alpha\) is a dimensionless constant to be fixed by energy conservation.

For a strong adiabatic shock with negligible upstream pressure,

\[p_1 = \frac{2}{\gamma+1}\rho_0 u_0^2.\]

The outward pressure force on the shell is then

\[F = 4\pi R^2 P_{\rm in} = 4\pi R^2 \alpha p_1.\]

Using the expression for \(p_1\), and writing the shell momentum in terms of \(u_0\), one obtains the approximate equation of motion

\[3\alpha R^2 u_0^2 = \frac{d}{dt}\left(u_0R^3\right).\]

Taking \(u_0 \sim \dot{R}\) for the purpose of determining the scaling, this gives

\[3\alpha R^2 \dot{R}^2 = \frac{d}{dt}\left(R^3\dot{R}\right) = 3R^2\dot{R}^2 + R^3\ddot{R}.\]

Now assume a power-law solution,

\[R(t) \propto t^\beta.\]

Substitution gives

\[\beta = \frac{1}{4 - 3\alpha}.\]

Therefore,

\[R(t) \propto t^{1/(4-3\alpha)}\]

and

\[\dot{R}(t) \propto t^{(3\alpha - 3)/(4-3\alpha)}.\]

To determine \(\alpha\), we impose energy conservation. The kinetic energy of the swept-up shell scales as

\[K \sim \rho_0 R^3 \dot{R}^2.\]

The internal energy of the hot post-shock gas is

\[U = \frac{1}{\gamma - 1} \int p\,dV.\]

In the thin-shell approximation, this also scales as

\[U \sim R^3 P_{\rm in} \sim R^3 \rho_0 \dot{R}^2.\]

Thus the total energy scales as

\[E_0 \sim \rho_0 R^3 \dot{R}^2.\]

Since \(E_0\) is conserved,

\[R^3\dot{R}^2 = \text{constant}.\]

For \(R \propto t^\beta\), this implies

\[t^{5\beta - 2} = \text{constant},\]

and therefore

\[\beta = \frac{2}{5}.\]

Hence the characteristic Sedov–Taylor scalings are

\[\boxed{ R(t) \propto t^{2/5} }\]

and

\[\boxed{ \dot{R}(t) \propto t^{-3/5} }.\]

Since the post-shock pressure scales as

\[p_1 \sim \rho_0 \dot{R}^2,\]

we also have

\[\boxed{ p_1(t) \propto t^{-6/5} }.\]

Dimensional Derivation#

The same result follows immediately from dimensional analysis. During the energy-conserving phase, the only dimensional quantities controlling the shock radius are

\[E_0, \qquad \rho_0, \qquad t.\]

Their dimensions are

\[[E_0] = M L^2 T^{-2}, \qquad [\rho_0] = M L^{-3}, \qquad [t] = T.\]

The unique combination with dimensions of length is

\[\left(\frac{E_0 t^2}{\rho_0}\right)^{1/5}.\]

Therefore the blast-wave radius must take the form

\[\boxed{ R(t) = \beta(\gamma) \left(\frac{E_0 t^2}{\rho_0}\right)^{1/5} }\]

where \(\beta(\gamma)\) is a dimensionless constant determined by the full similarity solution and depends only on \(\gamma\). The shock velocity is then

\[\boxed{ \dot{R}(t) = \frac{2}{5}\frac{R}{t} }.\]

Post-Shock Jump Conditions#

Immediately behind the shock, the Rankine–Hugoniot conditions for a strong shock give

\[\boxed{ \rho_1 = \rho_0 \frac{\gamma + 1}{\gamma - 1} }\]

and

\[\boxed{ p_1 = \frac{2}{\gamma + 1} \rho_0 \dot{R}^2 }.\]

The downstream gas velocity in the lab frame is

\[\boxed{ v_1 = \frac{2}{\gamma + 1}\dot{R} }.\]

These provide the boundary conditions for the self-similar flow just behind the shock.

Similarity Solution#

The full Sedov–Taylor solution resolves the spatial structure of the shocked gas behind the blast wave. In spherical symmetry, the adiabatic Euler equations are

\[\frac{\partial v}{\partial t} + v\frac{\partial v}{\partial r} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial p}{\partial r},\]
\[\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left(r^2\rho v\right) = 0,\]

and

\[\left( \frac{\partial}{\partial t} + v\frac{\partial}{\partial r} \right) \log\left(\frac{p}{\rho^\gamma}\right) = 0.\]

The last equation expresses adiabatic entropy conservation along streamlines behind the shock.

Because the problem contains no intrinsic length scale, the flow variables must depend on radius and time through the dimensionless similarity coordinate

\[\boxed{ \xi = \frac{r}{R(t)} }.\]

The shock front is located at \(\xi = 1\) and the center of the explosion at \(\xi = 0\). We write the hydrodynamic variables in self-similar form as

\[v(r,t) = \dot{R}(t) V(\xi),\]
\[\rho(r,t) = \rho_0 G(\xi),\]

and

\[p(r,t) = \rho_0 \dot{R}^2(t) P(\xi).\]

The functions \(V(\xi)\), \(G(\xi)\), and \(P(\xi)\) describe the dimensionless velocity, density, and pressure profiles inside the blast wave. At the shock, the Rankine–Hugoniot conditions impose the boundary conditions

\[V(1) = \frac{2}{\gamma+1}, \qquad G(1) = \frac{\gamma+1}{\gamma-1}, \qquad P(1) = \frac{2}{\gamma+1}.\]

Substituting the similarity ansatz into the Euler equations reduces the original partial differential equations to a coupled system of ordinary differential equations in \(\xi\). These ODEs are integrable in closed form for general \(\gamma\) (see Landau and Lifshitz[3], §106; Sedov[1]), yielding the explicit parametric solution described in the following section.

Normalization Coefficient#

The normalization \(\beta(\gamma)\) is not fixed by self-similarity alone; it is determined by requiring that the total energy contained within the blast equals the injected energy \(E_0\). This section derives the energy integral and documents the explicit similarity profiles used in its evaluation.

Energy Normalization Condition#

The total kinetic plus thermal energy inside the shock is

\[E_0 = 4\pi \int_0^{R(t)} \left[ \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \frac{p}{\gamma - 1} \right] r^2\,dr.\]

Substituting the similarity ansatz and changing the integration variable from \(r\) to \(\xi = r/R\), this becomes

\[E_0 = 4\pi \rho_0 \dot{R}^2 R^3 \int_0^1 \left[ \frac{1}{2}G(\xi)V^2(\xi) + \frac{P(\xi)}{\gamma - 1} \right] \xi^2\,d\xi.\]

Using the conventional notation from the similarity solution, it is more natural to replace the pressure profile \(P(\xi)\) with the dimensionless squared sound speed \(Z(\xi)\), defined by

\[c_s^2 = \frac{\gamma p}{\rho} \equiv \left(\frac{2r}{5t}\right)^2 Z(\xi),\]

so that \(P(\xi) = G(\xi) V(\xi)^2 Z(\xi) / \gamma\). The energy integral then reads

\[E_0 = \frac{16\pi}{25} \rho_0 \dot{R}^2 R^3 \underbrace{ \int_0^1 G(\xi) \left[ \frac{V(\xi)^2}{2} + \frac{Z(\xi)}{\gamma(\gamma-1)} \right] \xi^4\,d\xi }_{C_E(\gamma)/({16\pi}/{25})}.\]

Since \(\dot{R}^2 R^3 = (2R/5t)^2 R^3 = (4/25) R^5/t^2\), and \(R^5/t^2 = \beta^5 E_0/\rho_0\), the energy conservation condition reduces to

\[1 = \beta^5 C_E(\gamma),\]

hence

\[\boxed{ \beta(\gamma) = C_E(\gamma)^{-1/5}, \qquad C_E(\gamma) = \frac{16\pi}{25} \int_0^1 G(\xi) \left[ \frac{V(\xi)^2}{2} + \frac{Z(\xi)}{\gamma(\gamma-1)} \right] \xi^4\,d\xi. }\]

Note

Some references write \(R(t) = (E_0 t^2 / \alpha_{\rm ST} \rho_0)^{1/5}\), in which case \(\alpha_{\rm ST} = \beta^{-5} = C_E\). Both conventions are in common use; Triceratops uses the \(\beta\) form.

Explicit Parametric Profiles#

The similarity ODEs for \(V\), \(G\), and \(Z\) as functions of \(\xi\) are integrable in closed form. Following Sedov[1] and Landau and Lifshitz[3], the explicit solution uses the dimensionless velocity \(V\) as a parametric variable in place of \(\xi\). Define the auxiliary combinations

\[A \;=\; \tfrac{\gamma+1}{2}\,V, \qquad B \;=\; \frac{\gamma+1}{7-\gamma}\bigl[5 - (3\gamma-1)V\bigr],\]
\[C \;=\; \frac{\gamma+1}{\gamma-1}\,({\gamma V - 1}), \qquad D \;=\; \frac{\gamma+1}{\gamma-1}\,(1 - V),\]

and the five exponents

\[\nu_1 = -\frac{13\gamma^2 - 7\gamma + 12}{(3\gamma-1)(2\gamma+1)}, \qquad \nu_2 = \frac{5(\gamma-1)}{2\gamma+1}, \qquad \nu_3 = \frac{3}{2\gamma+1},\]
\[\nu_4 = -\frac{\nu_1}{2-\gamma}, \qquad \nu_5 = -\frac{2}{2-\gamma}.\]

Important

The formulae above are singular at \(\gamma = 2\), where several exponents diverge. Triceratops explicitly rejects this value.

The explicit parametric profiles are then

\[\xi(V) = \left(A^{-2} B^{\nu_1} C^{\nu_2}\right)^{1/5},\]
\[G(V) = \frac{\gamma+1}{\gamma-1}\,C^{\nu_3} B^{\nu_4} D^{\nu_5},\]
\[Z(V) = \frac{\gamma(\gamma-1)(1-V)\,V^2}{2(\gamma V - 1)}.\]

The parameter \(V\) ranges from

\[V_0 = \frac{1}{\gamma} \qquad\text{(origin,}\ \xi = 0\text{)}\]

to

\[V_s = \frac{2}{\gamma+1} \qquad\text{(shock,}\ \xi = 1\text{)}.\]

The lower endpoint \(V_0\) is numerically singular (several powers of \(C\) and \(D\) vanish there), and the integration begins at \(V_0 + \varepsilon\,\Delta V\) with a small offset \(\varepsilon\) to avoid the singularity.

Change of Variable for Numerical Integration#

Because the similarity profiles are expressed parametrically in \(V\), the energy integral is evaluated by changing the integration variable from \(\xi\) to \(V\):

\[C_E(\gamma) = \frac{16\pi}{25} \int_{V_0}^{V_s} G(V) \left[ \frac{V^2}{2} + \frac{Z(V)}{\gamma(\gamma-1)} \right] \xi(V)^4 \frac{d\xi}{dV}\,dV.\]

The Jacobian is obtained by differentiating \(\log\xi(V)\):

\[\frac{d\xi}{dV} = \xi(V) \times \frac{1}{5} \left[ -\frac{2}{V} \;-\; \frac{\nu_1(3\gamma-1)}{5-(3\gamma-1)V} \;+\; \frac{\nu_2\,\gamma}{\gamma V - 1} \right].\]

The integral is then evaluated numerically using scipy.integrate.quad(). For \(\gamma = 5/3\) (monatomic ideal gas),

\[\beta\!\left(\tfrac{5}{3}\right) \approx 1.1517, \qquad C_E\!\left(\tfrac{5}{3}\right) = \beta^{-5} \approx 2.026,\]

and for \(\gamma = 7/5 = 1.4\) (diatomic ideal gas),

\[\beta(1.4) \approx 1.033, \qquad C_E(1.4) \approx 1.18.\]

Note

sedov_taylor_beta() computes \(\beta(\gamma)\) once at class instantiation and caches it on the SedovTaylorShockEngine. The quadrature tolerances and the singularity offset \(\varepsilon\) are all user-controllable via keyword arguments.

Implementation Notes#

The derivations above are directly reflected in the Triceratops implementation:

  • sedov_taylor_beta() evaluates \(C_E(\gamma)\) via scipy.integrate.quad() over the parametric \(V\)-interval and returns \(\beta = C_E^{-1/5}\).

  • SedovTaylorShockEngine stores \(\beta\) and evaluates the shock kinematics

    \[R_s(t) = \beta\!\left(\frac{E_0\,t^2}{\rho_0}\right)^{1/5}, \qquad v_s(t) = \frac{2}{5}\frac{R_s}{t},\]

    together with the immediate post-shock thermodynamics from the strong-shock Rankine–Hugoniot conditions.

  • Post-shock temperature is computed from the ideal-gas relation

    \[T_s = \frac{p_s\,\mu\,m_p}{\rho_s\,k_B},\]

    where \(\mu\) is the mean molecular weight (in units of the proton mass \(m_p\)) and \(k_B\) is Boltzmann’s constant. For a fully ionized hydrogen plasma, \(\mu = 0.5\).
