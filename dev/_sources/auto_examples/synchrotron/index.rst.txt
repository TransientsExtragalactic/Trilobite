*****************************
Synchrotron Emission Examples
*****************************

This gallery covers the full chain of synchrotron physics in Trilobite — from the fundamental
kernel functions that describe single-electron emission, through spectral energy distribution
models with cooling and self-absorption, to the closure relations that connect observed SED
parameters back to physical source properties.

Examples are grouped into four progressive sections:

- **Fundamentals** — kernel functions, the ν–γ–B relation, and single-electron spectra
- **Spectral Energy Distributions** — SED model hierarchy, spectral regimes, and SSA
- **Electron Cooling** — synchrotron and inverse Compton cooling physics
- **Closures and Equipartition** — forward/inverse closure, emissivity scaling, and equipartition analysis

.. rubric:: Theory and API

For the underlying physics, see :ref:`synchrotron_theory` and :ref:`synch_sed_theory`.
For cooling theory, see :ref:`synchrotron_cooling_theory` and :ref:`electron_cooling`.
For the API, see :ref:`synchrotron_core`, :ref:`synchrotron_microphysics`, :ref:`synchrotron_seds`,
and :ref:`synchrotron_cooling`.

Synchrotron Fundamentals
========================

These examples explore the foundational building blocks of synchrotron radiation:
the kernel functions :math:`F(x)` and :math:`G(x)` that describe single-electron emission,
the central :math:`\nu \propto \gamma^2 B` frequency relation, and how a power-law electron
population produces a power-law radio spectrum.

These examples are the right starting point if you want to understand *why* synchrotron spectra
look the way they do before working with the higher-level SED classes.

**Theory:** :ref:`synchrotron_theory`

**API:** :ref:`synchrotron_core`

Spectral Energy Distributions
==============================

These examples demonstrate Trilobite' hierarchy of synchrotron SED models — from a simple
uncooled power-law spectrum through models that add radiative cooling and synchrotron
self-absorption (SSA). Each model class adds physical complexity and produces distinct
spectral shapes and break-frequency structures.

Use these examples to decide which SED class is appropriate for a given source and observing
configuration, and to understand how spectral slopes change across different regimes.

**Theory:** :ref:`synch_sed_theory`, :ref:`ssa`

**API:** :ref:`synchrotron_seds`, :ref:`synchrotron_microphysics`

Electron Cooling
================

These examples cover the radiative cooling of relativistic electrons in Trilobite:
synchrotron cooling rates, the time-evolving cooling Lorentz factor :math:`\gamma_c`,
the transition between fast- and slow-cooling regimes, and the role of inverse Compton
losses (quantified by the Compton :math:`Y` parameter) relative to synchrotron losses.

These examples are most relevant when modeling sources at late times (where cooling can
reshape the electron distribution) or when IC losses are non-negligible.

**Theory:** :ref:`synchrotron_cooling_theory`, :ref:`electron_cooling`

**API:** :ref:`synchrotron_cooling`

Closures and Equipartition
===========================

These examples demonstrate the *closure relations* that bridge the gap between observed SED
parameters (:math:`F_\mathrm{pk}`, :math:`\nu_\mathrm{pk}`) and the physical properties of
the emitting region (radius :math:`R`, magnetic field :math:`B`, electron density :math:`n_e`).

The **forward closure** maps physical parameters onto observables; the **inverse closure** does
the reverse. Together they enable a complete equipartition analysis: given a measured SED,
recover the source size, magnetic field, and energy content — and propagate measurement
uncertainties through to physical posteriors via MCMC.

**Theory:** :ref:`sed_forward_closure`, :ref:`sed_inverse_closure`, :ref:`synch_equipartition_theory`

**API:** :ref:`synchrotron_microphysics`, :ref:`synchrotron_seds`
