Opacity Theory#

Opacity quantifies how strongly a medium attenuates radiation. In Triceratops it appears in a variety of physical contexts: as the Rosseland mean opacity driving radiative diffusion in optically thick accretion disks, as a photon-absorption cross-section in spectral-transfer calculations, as the column-averaged opacity entering free-free optical-depth estimates for supernova CSM, and as a grey effective opacity in single-zone stellar-wind models. The triceratops.radiation.opacity module is deliberately context-agnostic: it provides a uniform evaluation interface so that the same opacity object can be dropped into any solver that needs \(\kappa(\rho, T)\), its logarithmic derivatives, or both.

This document reviews the physical basis of the opacity laws currently implemented in Triceratops and derives the power-law scalings and partial derivatives that enter iterative thermal solvers.

For usage instructions see Opacity Laws. For a guide to implementing new opacity laws see Developer Guide: Implementing Opacity Laws.

Definitions and Context#

The opacity \(\kappa\) (\(\mathrm{cm^2\,g^{-1}}\)) is defined as the attenuation cross-section per unit mass of the medium. For a plasma of density \(\rho\), the monochromatic absorption coefficient is

\[\alpha_\nu = \kappa_\nu\,\rho,\]

so the optical depth across a column of surface density \(\Sigma\) is \(\tau_\nu = \kappa_\nu\,\Sigma\).

Depending on the physical context, different frequency-averaged opacities are appropriate:

Mean

When to use

Rosseland mean \(\kappa_R\)

Radiative diffusion in optically thick media (accretion disk interiors, stellar envelopes). Harmonic average over \(\partial B_\nu/\partial T\).

Planck mean \(\kappa_P\)

Emission/absorption in optically thin media. Arithmetic average over \(B_\nu\).

Grey approximation \(\kappa\)

Single effective opacity independent of frequency; used when a simple effective description is sufficient or when the frequency dependence is not modelled.

The Rosseland mean is defined as

(1)#\[\frac{1}{\kappa_R} = \frac{\displaystyle\int_0^\infty \frac{1}{\kappa_\nu} \frac{\partial B_\nu}{\partial T}\,d\nu} {\displaystyle\int_0^\infty \frac{\partial B_\nu}{\partial T}\,d\nu},\]

which weights each frequency by the gradient of the Planck function. Because \(\partial B_\nu / \partial T\) peaks near \(h\nu \approx 3.83\,k_BT\), the Rosseland mean is dominated by the most transparent frequency channels (those with low \(\kappa_\nu\)) — a harmonic average that correctly captures photon diffusion in optically thick material.

The Planck mean is the arithmetic average weighted by the emission spectrum,

(2)#\[\kappa_P = \frac{\displaystyle\int_0^\infty \kappa_\nu\,B_\nu\,d\nu} {\displaystyle\int_0^\infty B_\nu\,d\nu},\]

where \(B_\nu(T) = (2h\nu^3/c^2)/(\exp(h\nu/k_BT)-1)\) is the Planck function. Unlike \(\kappa_R\), the Planck mean is dominated by the highest-opacity channels, making it appropriate for optically thin emission and radiative cooling calculations. By Jensen’s inequality \(\kappa_P \geq \kappa_R\) everywhere.

Opacity Laws in Triceratops#

All currently implemented opacity laws are Rosseland mean and live in triceratops.radiation.opacity.grey_opacity.rosseland. The module structure is:

triceratops.radiation.opacity
└── grey_opacity/          frequency-averaged (grey) opacities
    ├── rosseland/         Rosseland mean — all current implementations
    └── planck/            Planck mean — stub, ready for future work

Each class carries a mean_type attribute ("rosseland" for all current laws) so that solvers can distinguish opacity types at runtime without inspecting class identity. Planck mean implementations would set mean_type = "planck" and live in triceratops.radiation.opacity.grey_opacity.planck.

The top-level get_opacity() resolver exposes all registered laws by string key regardless of their location in the subpackage hierarchy.

Electron Scattering#

For a fully ionized plasma, electrons scatter photons via the Thomson process. The cross-section per electron is the Thomson cross-section \(\sigma_T = 6.652 \times 10^{-25}\) cm², which gives a mass opacity

(3)#\[\boxed{ \kappa_{\rm es} = \frac{\sigma_T}{m_p}\,\frac{1 + X}{2} \approx 0.34\,\mathrm{cm^2\,g^{-1}} }\]

for solar hydrogen mass fraction \(X = 0.70\). The factor \((1+X)/2\) arises because there are \((1+X)/2\) free electrons per proton mass in a fully ionized H/He mixture.

Electron scattering is a grey and constant opacity: it does not depend on density, temperature, or frequency at the photon energies relevant to accretion disks. It therefore dominates the opacity whenever the Kramers contribution (see below) is small, which occurs in low-density, high-temperature environments.

The partial derivatives are identically zero:

\[\frac{\partial \ln \kappa_{\rm es}}{\partial \ln \rho} = 0, \qquad \frac{\partial \ln \kappa_{\rm es}}{\partial \ln T} = 0.\]

Kramers Opacity Laws#

For a fully ionized plasma at temperatures \(T \lesssim 10^{7\text{–}8}\) K, photon–matter interactions are dominated by free–free (bremsstrahlung) absorption and bound–free (photoionization) absorption. Both processes share the same power-law dependence on density and temperature — a result known as Kramers’ law — and can therefore be combined into a single effective opacity.

The Kramers Power Laws#

The free–free Kramers opacity arises from inverse bremsstrahlung (stimulated emission of photons by free electrons in the Coulomb field of ions). Integrating the monochromatic free-free absorption coefficient over a Planck-weighted frequency average and expressing the result as a mass opacity gives Rybicki and Lightman[1]

(4)#\[\kappa_{\rm ff}(\rho, T) = \kappa_{\rm ff,0}\,\rho\,T^{-3.5}, \qquad \kappa_{\rm ff,0} \approx 3.68 \times 10^{22}\;\mathrm{cm^5\,g^{-2}\,K^{3.5}},\]

for solar composition without a Gaunt-factor correction.

The bound–free Kramers opacity arises from photoionization of partially or highly ionized atoms. The same Planck-weighted average yields the same power-law form but with a larger normalization Frank et al.[2]:

(5)#\[\kappa_{\rm bf}(\rho, T) = \kappa_{\rm bf,0}\,\rho\,T^{-3.5}, \qquad \kappa_{\rm bf,0} \approx 4.34 \times 10^{25}\;\mathrm{cm^5\,g^{-2}\,K^{3.5}}.\]

Because both processes share the exponents \((\rho^1, T^{-3.5})\), the combined Kramers opacity is

(6)#\[\kappa_{\rm ff+bf}(\rho, T) = (\kappa_{\rm ff,0} + \kappa_{\rm bf,0})\,\rho\,T^{-3.5} \equiv \kappa_{\rm Kr,0}\,\rho\,T^{-3.5},\]

where \(\kappa_{\rm Kr,0} = \kappa_{\rm ff,0} + \kappa_{\rm bf,0} \approx 4.34 \times 10^{25}\) cm⁵ g⁻² K³·⁵ (dominated by the bound-free term).

Physical Picture

The \(\rho\,T^{-3.5}\) scaling can be understood as follows. The absorption cross-section per electron–ion pair grows as \(\nu^{-3}\) (free–free) and the Boltzmann factor penalizes photons with \(h\nu \gg k_B T\). Integrating over a thermal photon distribution weighted by \(\partial B_\nu / \partial T\) selects photons near the peak of the Planck function at \(\nu_{\rm peak} \propto T\), yielding an effective cross-section \(\propto T^{-3.5}\). The density factor arises because the absorption rate is proportional to the density of both the absorber (electron) and target (ion).

Partial Derivatives for Pure Kramers#

Working in logarithmic space — as required by the Cython solvers — the partial derivatives are constant and exact:

(7)#\[\frac{\partial \ln \kappa_{\rm Kr}}{\partial \ln \rho} = 1, \qquad \frac{\partial \ln \kappa_{\rm Kr}}{\partial \ln T} = -3.5.\]

These values are independent of the normalization constant and hold for KramersFFOpacity, KramersBFOpacity, and KramersOpacity.

Combined Kramers and Electron Scattering#

The most physically complete grey opacity for a hot, fully ionized plasma includes both Thomson scattering and Kramers absorption. Because these are distinct processes that contribute additively to the total opacity,

(8)#\[\kappa(\rho, T) = \kappa_{\rm es} + \kappa_{\rm Kr,0}\,\rho\,T^{-3.5}.\]

Partial Derivatives for the Combined Form#

Let \(\kappa_{\rm Kr}(\rho,T) \equiv \kappa_{\rm Kr,0}\,\rho\,T^{-3.5}\) denote the Kramers contribution. Then

(9)#\[\frac{\partial \ln \kappa}{\partial \ln \rho} = \frac{\kappa_{\rm Kr}(\rho,T)}{\kappa(\rho,T)}, \qquad \frac{\partial \ln \kappa}{\partial \ln T} = -3.5\,\frac{\kappa_{\rm Kr}(\rho,T)}{\kappa(\rho,T)}.\]

These expressions hold for KramersFFESOpacity, KramersBFESOpacity, and KramersESOpacity. Note that the two partial derivatives are always related by

\[\frac{\partial \ln \kappa}{\partial \ln T} = -3.5\,\frac{\partial \ln \kappa}{\partial \ln \rho},\]

which provides a useful consistency check when implementing new combined-form laws.

Log-Space Representation#

The Cython-level disk solvers work entirely in natural-logarithm CGS space for numerical stability. For a function \(\kappa(\rho, T)\), the key quantities passed between Python and Cython are

\[\ln\kappa,\quad \frac{\partial\ln\kappa}{\partial\ln\rho},\quad \frac{\partial\ln\kappa}{\partial\ln T},\]

evaluated at \((\ln T,\,\ln\rho)\) rather than \((T,\,\rho)\). This choice ensures that the solver can represent opacity values spanning many decades (from \({\sim}10^{-7}\) to \({\sim}10^2\) cm² g⁻¹) without overflow or cancellation errors.

Table-based Rosseland Mean Opacity (OPAL)#

The OPAL project Badnell et al.[3] provides pre-computed Rosseland mean opacities for a wide range of stellar compositions. Triceratops ships a bundled table of 126 compositions from the Asplund & Grevesse (2005) solar mixture, covering

\[3.75 \le \log_{10}(T\,[\mathrm{K}]) \le 8.70, \qquad -8 \le \log_{10}(R) \le 1,\]

where \(R \equiv \rho / T_6^3\) and \(T_6 = 10^{-6}\,T\). Cells outside the physically valid region are marked NaN by the OPAL authors and are handled gracefully by the out-of-bounds strategy chosen at construction time.

Coordinate System#

OPAL tables are stored on a \((\log_{10}T,\,\log_{10}R)\) grid. The compressed variable \(R\) effectively normalises density by the temperature-dependent degeneracy threshold, making the valid table region approximately rectangular in \(\log_{10}T\)–\(\log_{10}R\) space even though the valid \(\log_{10}T\)–\(\log_{10}\rho\) region is highly irregular.

The coordinate transformation (applied inside the Cython kernel) is

\[\log_{10}R = \log_{10}\rho - 3\,(\log_{10}T - 6).\]

Triceratops also supports tables stored on a direct \((\log_{10}T,\,\log_{10}\rho)\) grid via the 'T_rho' coordinate system, in which no transformation is applied.

Bilinear Interpolation#

Within each rectangular cell of the \((\log_{10}T,\,\log_{10}R)\) grid, the Cython kernel C_OPALTableOpacity applies standard bilinear interpolation of \(\log_{10}\kappa\):

\[\log_{10}\kappa \approx (1-t_x)(1-t_y)\,k_{00} + t_x(1-t_y)\,k_{10} + (1-t_x)t_y\,k_{01} + t_x t_y\,k_{11},\]

where \(t_x = (\log_{10}T - g_1^{(i)}) / (g_1^{(i+1)} - g_1^{(i)})\) and similarly for \(t_y\). If any corner \(k_{ij}\) is NaN, the cell is treated as invalid and the out-of-bounds handler is invoked.

Log-space Derivatives#

Because the interpolation is piecewise bilinear, the log-space derivatives are piecewise constant within each cell and can be computed analytically from the cell corners:

\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial\ln\kappa}{\partial\ln\rho} = \frac{\partial\log_{10}\kappa}{\partial\log_{10}g_2}\\\begin{split}\frac{\partial\ln\kappa}{\partial\ln T}\bigg|_\rho = \begin{cases} \dfrac{\partial\log_{10}\kappa}{\partial\log_{10}T}\bigg|_R - 3\,\dfrac{\partial\log_{10}\kappa}{\partial\log_{10}R}\bigg|_T & \text{(T\_R system)} \\[6pt] \dfrac{\partial\log_{10}\kappa}{\partial\log_{10}T}\bigg|_\rho & \text{(T\_rho system)} \end{cases}\end{split}\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

The \(-3\) cross-term in the T_R system arises from the chain rule \(\partial\log_{10}R/\partial\log_{10}T\big|_\rho = -3\).

Out-of-bounds Strategies#

Queries outside the table domain (or landing in NaN cells) are handled by one of three strategies selected at construction time:

Mode

Behaviour

'raise'

Raises ValueError with the out-of-range coordinates (default; safest for debugging).

'clamp'

Returns the opacity at the nearest valid boundary point silently. Useful when the query point is expected to be near but occasionally outside the domain.

'nan'

Returns NaN silently. Useful for plotting or masking workflows where gaps are preferable to exceptions.
